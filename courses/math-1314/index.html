MATH 1314: Precalculus Algebra.

A public archive of a course built to help students move from symbolic manipulation to functional thinking. Design Idea

The course is organized around a simple arc: students first stabilize algebraic habits, then learn to treat functions as objects that can be represented, transformed, composed, inverted, modeled, and interpreted.

Video Policy

The original pages included some links to material maintained by other instructors. This polished archive points students toward the preserved pages with my YouTube lecture embeds and keeps third-party links out of the main course path.

Section 1

Introduction to Precalculus

Growth mindset and course architecture

Students begin by treating mathematical ability as something developed through deliberate practice, feedback, and revision. This frames the course as training in habits, not a one-time test of talent.

  • Identify how practice, feedback, and revision support mathematical learning.
  • Read a course objective and connect it to homework, exams, and class practice.
  • Use the archive as a self-study path rather than a pile of disconnected files.
Guiding principle: fluency grows when students move repeatedly among verbal, symbolic, graphical, and numerical representations.

Section 2

Equations and Inequalities

The Coordinate Plane

Students locate points, read geometric information from ordered pairs, and connect algebraic equations to graphical structure.

  • Plot and interpret points \((x,y)\) in the Cartesian plane.
  • Compute distance and midpoint using \(\sqrt{(x_2-x_1)^2+(y_2-y_1)^2}\) and \(\left(\frac{x_1+x_2}{2},\frac{y_1+y_2}{2}\right)\).
  • Recognize when a graph represents a solution set.
Example thread: turn two observed data points into a geometric story about change.
Videos 5-6

Linear Equations and Applications

Linear equations become the first setting where symbolic solution methods, graphing, and modeling all meet.

  • Solve equations equivalent to \(ax+b=cx+d\).
  • Distinguish one-solution, no-solution, and identity cases.
  • Write and solve a linear model from context.
Example thread: translate a cost or distance problem into \(mx+b=y\), then interpret the slope and intercept.

Complex Numbers, Quadratics, and Inequalities

The section closes by broadening what counts as a solution: complex roots, quadratic methods, higher-order equations, and interval descriptions.

  • Use \(i^2=-1\) to simplify complex expressions.
  • Solve quadratics by factoring, completing the square, and the quadratic formula.
  • Represent inequalities using interval notation and number-line reasoning.
Example thread: compare \(x^2-5x+6=0\), \(x^2+4=0\), and \(|x-2|<5\) as different kinds of solution sets.

Section 3

Functions

Notation, Domain, Range, and Rate of Change

Students learn to read function notation as a language for input, output, domain, range, and change.

  • Evaluate and interpret \(f(a)\), \(f(x+h)\), and \(\frac{f(b)-f(a)}{b-a}\).
  • Find domain restrictions caused by denominators, radicals, and context.
  • Estimate and interpret average rates of change from tables, graphs, and formulas.
Example thread: for \(f(x)=\sqrt{x-3}\), explain why the domain is \([3,\infty)\) before doing any calculation.
Videos 18-19Videos 20-21Videos 22-23

Composition, Transformations, Absolute Value, and Inverses

This is the central conceptual section: functions become objects students can combine, move, reflect, restrict, and reverse.

  • Compute and interpret \((f\circ g)(x)=f(g(x))\).
  • Describe transformations such as \(a f(b(x-h))+k\).
  • Find inverse functions by solving \(y=f(x)\) for \(x\), then checking \(f(f^{-1}(x))=x\).
Example thread: compare \(f(x)=|x|\), \(g(x)=|x-3|+2\), and \(h(x)=-2|x+1|\) as transformations of one parent function.

Section 4

Linear Functions and Models

Linear Functions

Students consolidate slope, intercepts, equations of lines, and linear modeling into a single representational system.

  • Move among slope-intercept, point-slope, and standard forms.
  • Interpret \(m\) and \(b\) in \(f(x)=mx+b\).
  • Use linear models for prediction while naming the limits of extrapolation.
Example thread: fit a line through two points and explain what one unit of horizontal change means in context.

Section 5

Polynomials, Rational Functions, and Radicals

Quadratics and Polynomial Graphs

Students learn to read algebraic features as graphical features: zeros, multiplicity, end behavior, turning points, and intercepts.

  • Analyze \(f(x)=ax^2+bx+c\) using vertex, intercepts, and concavity.
  • Use factored form \(a(x-r_1)(x-r_2)\) to locate zeros.
  • Predict end behavior from degree and leading coefficient.
Example thread: sketch \(f(x)=-(x-2)^2+5\) and explain every visible feature from the formula.
Videos 35-36Videos 37-38Videos 39-40

Division, Zeros, Rational Functions, and Radicals

The algebra becomes more structural: students use division algorithms, factor theorems, asymptotes, and radical domains to understand families of functions.

  • Divide polynomials using long division or synthetic division.
  • Use the remainder and factor theorems to test candidate zeros.
  • Describe vertical/horizontal asymptotes and holes of rational functions.
  • Solve radical equations while checking for extraneous solutions.
Example thread: compare \(\frac{x^2-1}{x-1}\) and \(\frac{1}{x-1}\): both have \(x=1\) excluded, but the graphs behave differently.

Section 6

Exponential and Logarithmic Functions

Exponential Growth and Logarithmic Inverses

The course ends by connecting growth, inverse functions, graphs, symbolic properties, equations, and models.

  • Interpret \(f(x)=ab^x\) using initial value \(a\) and growth factor \(b\).
  • Use \(\log_b(x)=y\) exactly when \(b^y=x\).
  • Apply logarithm properties to expand, condense, and solve equations.
  • Model growth and decay with exponential functions.
Example thread: solve \(2e^{0.4t}=10\) by isolating the exponential and applying \(\ln\) to both sides.
