Integration by Parts

Calculus 2 • Notes • Friday 3-27
Learning Objectives
  • Use integration by parts to find an antiderivative when standard substitution fails.
  • Derive the integration by parts formula from the product rule.
  • Evaluate both indefinite and definite integrals using the parts formula.

1. Deriving the Formula

Notice that the integral of a product is not the product of the integrals:

$$ \int f(x)g(x) \,dx \neq \int f(x) \,dx \int g(x) \,dx $$

Instead, we start with The Product Rule for derivatives:

$$ (f(x)g(x))' = f(x)g'(x) + g(x)f'(x) $$

Writing this in integral form (integrating both sides with respect to $x$):

$$ \int \big[ f(x)g'(x) + g(x)f'(x) \big] \,dx = f(x)g(x) $$
$$ \int f(x)g'(x) \,dx + \int g(x)f'(x) \,dx = f(x)g(x) + C $$

Rearranging the terms gives us the basis for our formula:

$$ \int f(x)g'(x) \,dx = f(x)g(x) - \int g(x)f'(x) \,dx $$

To make this easier to remember, we use substitution. Let:

$u = f(x)$ $du = f'(x) \,dx$
$v = g(x)$ $dv = g'(x) \,dx$

Integration by Parts Formula

$$ \int u \,dv = uv - \int v \,du $$

Steps for Integration by Parts

*You might need to repeat these steps multiple times for certain integrals!
  1. Pick $u$ and identify $dv$.
  2. Compute $du$ by differentiating $u$.
  3. Compute $v$ by integrating $dv$.
  4. Plug into the formula and evaluate the new integral.

2. Examples

Example 1: Indefinite Integral

Evaluate $\int (2x+1)\cos(x) \,dx$

$u = 2x + 1$ ↓ derivative $du = 2 \,dx$
$dv = \cos(x) \,dx$ ↓ integrate $v = \sin(x)$
$$ \int (2x+1)\cos(x) \,dx = \underbrace{(2x+1)}_{u}\underbrace{\sin(x)}_{v} - \int \underbrace{\sin(x)}_{v} \cdot \underbrace{2 \,dx}_{du} $$ $$ = (2x+1)\sin(x) - 2 \int \sin(x) \,dx $$ $$ = (2x+1)\sin(x) - 2(-\cos(x)) + C $$
$= (2x+1)\sin(x) + 2\cos(x) + C$
Example 2: Involving Natural Log

Evaluate $\int (x^2 + 2x + 1)\ln(x) \,dx$

$u = \ln(x)$ $du = \frac{1}{x} \,dx$
$dv = (x^2 + 2x + 1) \,dx$ $v = \frac{x^3}{3} + x^2 + x$
$$ \int (x^2+2x+1)\ln(x) \,dx = \ln(x)\left(\frac{x^3}{3} + x^2 + x\right) - \int \left(\frac{x^3}{3} + x^2 + x\right) \cdot \frac{1}{x} \,dx $$

Distribute the $\frac{1}{x}$ inside the integral to simplify:

$$ = \ln(x)\left(\frac{x^3}{3} + x^2 + x\right) - \int \left(\frac{x^2}{3} + x + 1\right) \,dx $$
$= \ln(x)\left(\frac{x^3}{3} + x^2 + x\right) - \left(\frac{x^3}{9} + \frac{x^2}{2} + x\right) + C$
Example 3: Definite Integral

Evaluate $\int_1^2 5x\sin(x) \,dx$

$u = 5x$ $du = 5 \,dx$
$dv = \sin(x) \,dx$ $v = -\cos(x)$

When applying limits, remember to evaluate $uv$ across the bounds as well:

$$ \int_1^2 5x\sin(x) \,dx = \left. 5x(-\cos(x)) \right|_1^2 - \int_1^2 -\cos(x) \cdot 5 \,dx $$ $$ = \big(-10\cos(2) - (-5\cos(1))\big) + 5 \int_1^2 \cos(x) \,dx $$ $$ = \big(-10\cos(2) + 5\cos(1)\big) + \left. 5\sin(x) \right|_1^2 $$ $$ = \big(-10\cos(2) + 5\cos(1)\big) + \big(5\sin(2) - 5\sin(1)\big) $$
$\approx 7.202$
