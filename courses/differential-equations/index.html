Ordinary Differential Equations

A course in models, methods, and systems. The workbook is organized into cycles that combine interactive notes, structured classwork, homework, and quizzes; this page explains the mathematical content covered.

All Courses Full Workbook

1. First-Order Equations and Numerical Methods

The course begins by defining ordinary differential equations, solutions, initial value problems, direction fields, separable equations, first-order linear equations, and Euler's method.

Objectives

  • Verify solutions by substitution.
  • Solve separable and first-order linear equations.
  • Use direction fields and Euler's method to understand solution behavior.

Cycles

Cycles 1-2 cover separable equations, geometric analysis, Euler's method, and first-order linear equations.

Representative example: solve a Newton's-law-of-cooling IVP and interpret the long-term temperature predicted by the model.

2. Second-Order Equations and Physical Models

Students move to models with acceleration and stored energy. Existence theory, mass-spring-damper systems, RLC circuits, and homogeneous second-order equations connect algebraic solution methods to physical behavior.

Objectives

  • State the role of existence and uniqueness.
  • Solve homogeneous constant-coefficient equations.
  • Interpret overdamped, critically damped, and underdamped behavior.

Cycles

Cycles 3-4 cover existence, mass-spring-damper models, RLC circuits, and real, complex, or repeated characteristic roots.

Representative example: solve \(ay''+by'+cy=0\) from its characteristic equation and describe whether the solution oscillates.

3. Nonhomogeneous Equations and Undetermined Coefficients

External forcing enters through nonhomogeneous equations. Students learn the method of undetermined coefficients, including polynomial, exponential, sine/cosine, product forcing, and the modification rule.

Objectives

  • Find complementary and particular solutions.
  • Choose a trial form from the forcing term.
  • Apply the modification rule when the trial overlaps the homogeneous solution.

Cycles

Cycles 5-7 cover nonhomogeneous practice, undetermined coefficients, products of functions, forced oscillations, resonance, beats, and damping.

Representative example: solve \(y''+y=\cos t\) and explain why the naive trial solution must be modified.

4. The Laplace Transform

The Laplace transform changes differential equations into algebraic equations. Students use transform tables, derivative rules, translation theorems, inverse transforms, Heaviside functions, Dirac delta inputs, and convolution.

Objectives

  • Compute transforms and inverse transforms.
  • Solve IVPs using operational properties.
  • Model discontinuous or impulsive forcing.

Cycles

Cycles 7-9 cover the definition of the Laplace transform, derivative properties, shifting, multiplication by \(t\), inverse transforms, Heaviside functions, delta functions, and convolution.

Representative example: transform an IVP, solve for \(Y(s)\), use partial fractions, then invert to recover \(y(t)\).

5. Systems of Differential Equations

The course closes with systems. Students study linear systems, eigenvalue methods, matrix exponentials, phase portraits, and the qualitative behavior of solutions in the plane.

Objectives

  • Write systems in matrix form.
  • Solve systems with distinct, complex, or repeated eigenvalues.
  • Sketch and interpret phase portraits.

Cycles

Cycles 10-12 cover linear systems, eigenvalue methods, matrix exponentials, and phase portraits.

Representative example: classify the equilibrium of a \(2\times2\) linear system from its eigenvalues and sketch the qualitative flow.
