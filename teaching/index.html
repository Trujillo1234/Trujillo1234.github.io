Philosophy

I want students to experience mathematics as something they can enter, test, revise, explain, and eventually own. That requires more than clear lectures. It requires a course structure where the pieces visibly fit together: learning objectives, examples, classwork, homework, quizzes, exams, written reflection, and feedback should all point toward the same mathematical habits.

My best courses are built from backward design and active learning. I begin with the mathematical actions students should be able to perform: compute, define, justify, model, compare, prove, read, and explain. Then I build the daily work so students practice those actions repeatedly, with increasing independence. The goal is not to make a class feel easy; it is to make the route through a hard subject visible enough that students can keep moving.

I also believe students should see that mathematics has a human voice. They need polished notation and precise arguments, but they also need chances to ask messy questions, make partial attempts, argue from examples, and learn what a proof or calculation is trying to say. The classroom should be structured enough to protect serious work and generous enough to let students grow into it.

Course Design

Much of my recent teaching work has focused on courses as systems. In calculus II, calculus III, differential equations, and logic, I have built or revised public materials that connect notes, examples, classwork, homework, videos, quizzes, and review. These materials are not meant to replace class; they are meant to make the class legible before, during, and after the meeting.

In Algebraic Structures I used standards-based grading around 42 specific learning objectives, combining written proofs, presentations, portfolios, oral exams, quizzes, and exams. The purpose was to make progress visible and to give students multiple serious ways to demonstrate mathematical understanding.

Evidence and Reflection

My recent teaching award packet gathered student evaluations, letters, course redesign work, and mentoring examples. Across five semesters and thirteen course sections, the IDEA summary reported a 91.6 percent response rate and a weighted mean of 4.92 out of 5 for excellent teacher. I do not take those numbers as a substitute for craft, but they are a useful sign that students often experience the structure, clarity, and feedback as meaningful.

The letters in that packet describe the same pattern from another angle: students remember courses that were demanding but navigable, and mentoring relationships where independent work became possible because the expectations were clear enough to support risk.

Mentoring

I have mentored students in mathematical logic, graph theory, chaos theory, category theory, combinatorics, generalized trigonometric systems, mathematical modeling, and foundations. That work has led to conference presentations, graduate capstones, independent studies, undergraduate research projects, and ongoing mathematical conversations that outlast a single course.
