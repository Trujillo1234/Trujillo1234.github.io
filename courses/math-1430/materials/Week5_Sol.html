Calculus 2 • Series Convergence, Power Series, & Taylor Series

1. Series Convergence

Determine if the series converges or diverges. Use any test.
  1. $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{2^n}{3^n+n} $$
    Solution 1: Direct Comparison Test For all $n>0$, $$ 0<\frac{2^n}{3^n+n}\le \frac{2^n}{3^n}=\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^n. $$ The geometric series $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^n$ converges since $r=\frac{2}{3}$ and $|r|<1$.

    By the direct comparison test, $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{2^n}{3^n+n}$ must also converge.
    Solution 2: Limit Comparison Test Compare to the geometric series $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^n$. $$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\frac{2^n}{3^n+n}}{\left(\frac{2}{3}\right)^n} =\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\frac{2^n}{3^n+n}}{\frac{2^n}{3^n}} =\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{3^n}{3^n+n}. $$ Using L'Hôpital's Rule: $$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{3^n}{3^n+n} \xrightarrow{L'H}\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\ln(3)\,3^n}{\ln(3)\,3^n+1} \xrightarrow{L'H}\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\ln(3)\ln(3)\,3^n}{\ln(3)\ln(3)\,3^n}=1. $$ Since $0<1<\infty$, the limit comparison test implies that $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{2^n}{3^n+n}$ must also converge.
  2. $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{2}{n^2+n} $$
    Solution 1: Direct Comparison Test For all $n\ge 1$, $$ 0<\frac{2}{n^2+n}\le \frac{2}{n^2}=2\cdot\frac{1}{n^2}. $$ The series $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^2}$ is a $p$-series with $p=2$ and $p\ge 1$, so it converges.

    By the direct comparison test, $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{2}{n^2+n}$ must also converge.
    Solution 2: Limit Comparison Test Compare to $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^2}$. $$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\frac{2}{n^2+n}}{\frac{1}{n^2}} =\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{2}{n^2+n}\cdot n^2 =\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{2n^2}{n^2+n}. $$ Using L'Hôpital's Rule: $$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{2n^2}{n^2+n} \xrightarrow{L'H}\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{4n}{2n+1} \xrightarrow{L'H}\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{4}{2}=2. $$ Since $0<2<\infty$, the limit comparison test shows that $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{2}{n^2+n}$ must also converge.
  3. $$ \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{\sqrt{n}}{n^2+n} $$
    Solution 1: Direct Comparison Test For all $n>0$, $$ 0<\frac{\sqrt{n}}{n^2+n}\le \frac{\sqrt{n}}{n^2}=\frac{1}{n^{1.5}}. $$ The series $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^{1.5}}$ is a $p$-series with $p=1.5$, and since $p=1.5>1$ it converges.

    By the direct comparison test, $\displaystyle\sum_{n=2}^{\infty}\frac{\sqrt{n}}{n^2+n}$ must also converge.
    Solution 2: Limit Comparison Test Compare to $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^{1.5}}$. $$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\frac{\sqrt{n}}{n^2+n}}{\frac{1}{n^{1.5}}} =\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\sqrt{n}}{n^2+n}\cdot n^{1.5} =\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n^2}{n^2+n}. $$ Using L'Hôpital's Rule: $$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n^2}{n^2+n} \xrightarrow{L'H}\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{2n}{2n+1}=1. $$ Since $0<1<\infty$, the limit comparison test implies that $\displaystyle\sum_{n=2}^{\infty}\frac{\sqrt{n}}{n^2+n}$ must also converge.
  4. $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{n^2+n} $$
    Alternating Series Test Let $b_n=\dfrac{1}{n^2+n}$.
    1. For all $n>0$, $b_n=\dfrac{1}{n^2+n}>0$.
    2. For all $n>0$, $$ b_{n+1}=\frac{1}{(n+1)^2+(n+1)}\le \frac{1}{n^2+n}=b_n. $$
    3. $$ \lim_{n\to\infty} b_n=\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n^2+n}=0. $$
    Since (1), (2), and (3) are all true, the alternating series test implies that the series converges.
    Also possible to use absolute convergence with a comparison test.
  5. $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{n+1}{3n+2} $$
    Divergence Test $$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n+1}{3n+2} \xrightarrow{L'H}\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{3}=\frac{1}{3}\ne 0. $$ Since the limit of the terms is not $0$, the divergence test implies that $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{n+1}{3n+2}$ diverges.
  6. $$ \sum_{n=3}^{\infty} \frac{n+1}{n^2-2n} $$
    Solution 1: Limit Comparison Test Compare to the $p$-series $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n}$. This series diverges since $p=1$ and $p\le 1$. $$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\frac{n+1}{n^2-2n}}{\frac{1}{n}} =\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n+1}{n^2-2n}\cdot n =\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n^2+n}{n^2-2n}. $$ Using L'Hôpital's Rule: $$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n^2+n}{n^2-2n} \xrightarrow{L'H}\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{2n+1}{2n-2} \xrightarrow{L'H}\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{2}{2}=1. $$ Since $0<1<\infty$, the limit comparison test implies that $\displaystyle\sum_{n=3}^{\infty}\frac{n+1}{n^2-2n}$ must also diverge.
    Solution 2: Direct Comparison Test For all $n>3$, $$ 0<\frac{1}{n}=\frac{n}{n^2}\le \frac{n+1}{n^2-2n}. $$ The series $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n}$ diverges (it is a $p$-series with $p=1$ and $p\le 1$).

    By the direct comparison test, $\displaystyle\sum_{n=3}^{\infty}\frac{n+1}{n^2-2n}$ must also diverge.
  7. $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \cos\left(\frac{1}{n}\right) $$
    Divergence Test $$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\cos\left(\frac{1}{n}\right) =\cos\left(\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}\right) =\cos(0)=1\ne 0. $$ Since $1\ne 0$, the divergence test implies that $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\cos\!\left(\frac{1}{n}\right)$ diverges.
  8. $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt[5]{n}} $$
    p-Series Test Note that $$ \frac{1}{\sqrt[5]{n}}=\frac{1}{n^{1/5}}. $$ So the series is a $p$-series with $p=\frac{1}{5}$. Since $\frac{1}{5}\le 1$, the series diverges by the $p$-series test.
  9. $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{n^5}{n^6+5n} $$
    Solution 1: Direct Comparison Test For all $n>5$, $$ 0<\frac{n^5}{n^6+5n}\le \frac{n^5}{n^6}=\frac{1}{n}. $$ The series $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n}$ is a constant multiple of a $p$-series with $p=1$, and since $p\le 1$, it diverges.

    By the direct comparison test, $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{n^5}{n^6+5n}$ must also diverge.
    Solution 2: Limit Comparison Test Compare to the $p$-series $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n}$ (divergent since $p=1\le 1$). $$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\frac{n^5}{n^6+5n}}{\frac{1}{n}} =\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n^5}{n^6+5n}\cdot n =\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n^6}{n^6+5n}. $$ Using L'Hôpital's Rule: $$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n^6}{n^6+5n} \xrightarrow{L'H}\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{6n^5}{6n^5+5} \xrightarrow{L'H}\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{30n^4}{30n^4}=1. $$ Since $0<1<\infty$, the limit comparison test implies that $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{n^5}{n^6+5n}$ must also diverge.
  10. $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} n^2 $$
    Divergence Test $$ \lim_{n\to\infty} n^2=\infty\ne 0. $$ Since the limit is not $0$, the divergence test implies that $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}n^2$ diverges.
    Note: This is also a $p$-series with $p=-2$. Thus, the $p$-series test also shows the series diverges since $p\le 1$.
  11. $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{2n+1} $$
    Alternating Series Test Let $b_n=\dfrac{1}{2n+1}$.
    1. For all $n>0$, $b_n=\dfrac{1}{2n+1}>0$.
    2. For all $n>0$, $$ b_{n+1}=\frac{1}{2n+3}\le \frac{1}{2n+1}=b_n. $$
    3. $$ \lim_{n\to\infty} b_n=\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{2n+1}=0. $$
    Since (1), (2), and (3) are all true, the alternating series test shows that $\displaystyle\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{2n+1}$ converges.
    Note: It is also possible to do this using absolute convergence with a comparison test.
  12. $$ \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{5n^2+3n}{7n^2-n} $$
    Divergence Test $$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{5n^2+3n}{7n^2-n} \xrightarrow{L'H}\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{10n+3}{14n-1} \xrightarrow{L'H}\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{10}{14}=\frac{5}{7}\ne 0. $$ Since $\frac{5}{7}\ne 0$, the divergence test shows that $\displaystyle\sum_{n=2}^{\infty}\frac{5n^2+3n}{7n^2-n}$ diverges.

2. Power Series

Find the radius and center of convergence for each series.
  1. $$ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} n! (x-10)^n $$
    Center: $a=10$.

    Let $a_n=n!$. (So the series is $\sum a_n (x-10)^n$.)

    Ratio Test (on coefficients): $$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| =\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{(n+1)!}{n!} =\lim_{n\to\infty}(n+1)=\infty. $$ Therefore the radius of convergence is $\boxed{R=0}$.
  2. $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{2^n}{n} (x-5)^n $$
    Center: $a=5$.

    Let $a_n=\dfrac{2^n}{n}$.

    Ratio Test (on coefficients): $$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| =\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\frac{2^{n+1}}{n+1}}{\frac{2^n}{n}} =\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{2^{n+1}}{n+1}\cdot\frac{n}{2^n} =\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{2n}{n+1}=2. $$ Therefore the radius of convergence is $\boxed{R=\frac{1}{2}}$.
  3. $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{x^n}{n^2} $$
    Center: $a=0$.

    Let $a_n=\dfrac{1}{n^2}$.

    Ratio Test (on coefficients): $$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| =\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\frac{1}{(n+1)^2}}{\frac{1}{n^2}} =\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n^2}{(n+1)^2}. $$ Using L'Hôpital's Rule: $$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n^2}{(n+1)^2} \xrightarrow{L'H}\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{2n}{2(n+1)} \xrightarrow{L'H}\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{2}{2}=1. $$ Therefore the radius of convergence is $\boxed{R=1}$.

3. Taylor Series

Find the Taylor series at $x=0$ (Maclaurin series) for each function.
  1. $$ f(x) = e^{-x} $$
    $$ e^x=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{x^n}{n!} \quad\Longrightarrow\quad e^{-x}=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-x)^n}{n!} =\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^n x^n}{n!}. $$
  2. $$ g(x) = \frac{1}{1-5x} $$
    $$ \frac{1}{1-5x}=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(5x)^n =\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}5^n x^n. $$
  3. $$ h(x) = \sin(-x) $$
    $$ \sin(-x)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^n(-x)^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!} =\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^n(-1)\,x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!} =\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^{n+1}x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}. $$
    (Here we used $(-1)^{2n+1}=-1$.)
  4. $$ k(x) = \frac{1}{1-x^2} $$
    $$ \frac{1}{1-x^2}=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}(x^2)^n=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}x^{2n}. $$
  5. $$ l(x) = \cos(x^3) $$
    $$ \cos(x^3)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^n(x^3)^{2n}}{(2n)!} =\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^n x^{6n}}{(2n)!}. $$

4. Taylor Polynomials

Find the Taylor polynomials as indicated below.
  1. $$ f(x)=5^x \quad \text{at } x=0 $$
    $f(x)=5^x \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad f(0)=5^0=1$
    $f'(x)=\ln(5)\,5^x \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad f'(0)=\ln(5)\,5^0=\ln(5)$
    $f''(x)=\ln(5)\ln(5)\,5^x \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad f''(0)=\ln(5)\ln(5)\,5^0=(\ln 5)^2$
    $$ P_1(x)=1+\ln(5)\,x $$ $$ P_2(x)=1+\ln(5)\,x+\frac{(\ln 5)^2}{2}\,x^2 $$
  2. $$ f(x)=x^5 \quad \text{at } x=1 $$
    $f(x)=x^5 \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad f(1)=1^5=1$
    $f'(x)=5x^4 \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad f'(1)=5\cdot 1^4=5$
    $f''(x)=20x^3 \qquad\Rightarrow\qquad f''(1)=20\cdot 1^3=20$
    $$ P_1(x)=1+5(x-1) $$ $$ P_2(x)=1+5(x-1)+\frac{20}{2}(x-1)^2 $$
  3. $$ f(x)=\tan(x) \quad \text{at } x=\frac{\pi}{4} $$
    $f\!\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right)=\tan\!\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right)=1$
    $f'(x)=\sec^2(x)\qquad\Rightarrow\qquad f'\!\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right)=\sec^2\!\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right)=(\sqrt{2})^2=2$
    $f''(x)=2\sec(x)\cdot \sec(x)\tan(x)=2\sec^2(x)\tan(x)$
    $f''\!\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right)=2\sec^2\!\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right)\tan\!\left(\frac{\pi}{4}\right)=2(2)(1)=4$
    $$ P_1(x)=1+2\left(x-\frac{\pi}{4}\right) $$ $$ P_2(x)=1+2\left(x-\frac{\pi}{4}\right)+\frac{4}{2}\left(x-\frac{\pi}{4}\right)^2 $$
