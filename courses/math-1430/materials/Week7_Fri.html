1. The Net Change Theorem

Theorem

The new value of a changing quantity equals the initial value plus the integral of the rate of change:

$$ f(b) = f(a) + \int_a^b f'(x) \, dx $$

or

$$ \underbrace{f(b) - f(a)}_{\substack{\text{Net change} \\ \text{of } f(x) \\ \text{on } [a,b]}} = \int_a^b f'(x) \, dx $$

2. Applications & Examples

Example 1: Particle Motion

The velocity of a particle is $v(t) = 3t - 5$ meters per second. Find the net displacement from $t=0$ to $t=3$ sec.

Let $s(t)$ denote the displacement function. Then $s'(t) = v(t)$.

$$ \text{Net displacement on } [0,3] = \int_0^3 (3t - 5) \, dt $$ $$ = \left[ 3\frac{t^2}{2} - 5t \right]_0^3 $$ $$ = \left( \frac{3(3)^2}{2} - 5(3) \right) - (0 - 0) $$ $$ = \left( \frac{27}{2} - 15 \right) $$ $$ = 13.5 - 15 = -1.5 \text{ meters} $$
Example 2: Fuel Consumption

A boat consumes gasoline at a rate of $r(t) = 5 - \frac{t^3}{10}$ gallons per hour. How much gasoline is used in the first 2 hours?

Suppose that $V(t)$ denotes the volume of gasoline in gallons at time $t$ measured in hours. Then $V'(t) = r(t)$.

$$ \text{Net change in } V(t) \text{ over } [0,2] = \int_0^2 \left( 5 - \frac{t^3}{10} \right) \, dt $$ $$ = \left[ 5t - \frac{t^4}{40} \right]_0^2 $$ $$ = \left( 5(2) - \frac{2^4}{40} \right) - (0 - 0) $$ $$ = 10 - \frac{16}{40} = 10 - 0.4 $$ $$ = 9.6 \text{ gallons} $$
Example 3: Calorie Burning

Sandra weighs 120 lbs. She burns calories at $r(t) = 300 - 50t$ calories per minute while walking. What is her net decrease in calories after walking for 3 hours?

$$ \text{Net Change} = \int_0^3 (300 - 50t) \, dt $$ $$ = \left[ 300t - 50\frac{t^2}{2} \right]_0^3 $$ $$ = \left( 300(3) - 25(3)^2 \right) - (0 - 0) $$ $$ = 900 - 225 $$ $$ = 675 \text{ calories} $$

3. Integral Formulas

$$ \int \cos(x) \, dx = \sin(x) + C $$ $$ \int \sin(x) \, dx = -\cos(x) + C $$ $$ \int \tan(x) \, dx = \ln|\sec(x)| + C $$ $$ \int \frac{1}{x} \, dx = \ln|x| + C $$
$$ \int x^n \, dx = \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C \quad (n \neq -1) $$ $$ \int e^x \, dx = e^x + C $$ $$ \int \sec^2(x) \, dx = \tan(x) + C $$ $$ \int \sec(x)\tan(x) \, dx = \sec(x) + C $$
Note: Use $\ln|x|$ when $n = -1$

4. More Examples

Example: Population Growth

Suppose that a population of flies increases at a rate of $g(t) = 2e^{0.2t}$ flies per day. If the initial population is 100 flies, how many flies are there after 10 days?

Let $P(t)$ be the population of flies at time $t$ measured in days. Then $P'(t) = g(t)$.
Using the Net Change Theorem: $$ P(10) = P(0) + \int_0^{10} 2e^{0.2t} \, dt $$ $$ = 100 + \int_0^{10} 2e^{0.2t} \, dt $$
U-Substitution
$u = 0.2t$
$du = 0.2 \, dt \implies 5 \, du = dt$
$u(0) = 0.2(0) = 0$
$u(10) = 0.2(10) = 2$
$$ = 100 + \int_0^2 2e^u \cdot 5 \, du $$ $$ = 100 + 10 \int_0^2 e^u \, du $$ $$ = 100 + 10 \left[ e^u \right]_0^2 $$ $$ = 100 + 10(e^2 - e^0) $$ $$ = 100 + 10e^2 - 10 $$ $$ \approx 164 \text{ flies} $$ (Note: handwritten notes approximated to 122, likely due to a typo in the exponent $0.02t$ vs $0.2t$)
Example: Definite Integral with Trig

Evaluate $\displaystyle \int_0^{\pi/2} \frac{\sin(x)}{1+\cos(x)} \, dx$

U-Substitution
$u = 1 + \cos(x)$
$du = -\sin(x) \, dx$
$u(0) = 1 + \cos(0) = 2$
$u(\pi/2) = 1 + \cos(\pi/2) = 1$
$$ = \int_2^1 \frac{1}{u} (-du) = \int_1^2 \frac{1}{u} \, du $$ $$ = \ln|u| \Big|_1^2 $$ $$ = \ln|2| - \ln|1| $$ $$ = \ln(2) $$
