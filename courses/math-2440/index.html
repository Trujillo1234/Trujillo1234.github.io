Coursebook

Calculus III

A multivariable calculus course organized into cycles. Each cycle introduces a coherent block of geometry, computation, classwork, homework, and quiz preparation; the public page below explains the mathematical content covered by the workbook.

All Courses Full Course Packet Final Objectives

1. Parametric Motion and Polar Coordinates

The course begins by separating motion from the shape of a curve. Parametric equations describe a particle moving through the plane, while polar coordinates provide a second language for curves built from angle and distance.

Objectives

  • Interpret \(x=f(t)\), \(y=g(t)\) as both motion and geometry.
  • Eliminate parameters when possible.
  • Compute velocity, speed, arc length, and polar area.

Cycles

Cycle 1 covers parametric equations, calculus of parametric curves, polar graphs, and polar area.

Representative example: compare two parametrizations of the same circle and explain how the path can be identical while speed or orientation changes.

2. Vectors, Dot Products, Cross Products, Lines, and Planes

Vectors turn geometry into algebra. Students use dot products for projection and angle, cross products for area and normal vectors, and vector equations to describe lines and planes in three-dimensional space.

Objectives

  • Compute and interpret dot products and cross products.
  • Find equations of lines and planes.
  • Analyze intersections and distances in space.

Cycles

Cycles 2-4 cover vectors, product geometry, planes, lines, and interactions among lines and planes.

Representative example: use the cross product of two direction vectors to find a plane normal, then write the equation of the plane through a given point.

3. Vector-Valued Functions, Curvature, and the TNB Frame

The course returns to motion in space using vector-valued functions. Students connect velocity, acceleration, curvature, unit tangent, normal vectors, and acceleration components.

Objectives

  • Differentiate and integrate vector-valued functions.
  • Compute tangent and normal vectors.
  • Decompose acceleration into tangential and normal components.

Cycles

Cycles 4-5 develop calculus of vector-valued functions, curvature, the normal vector, and the TNB frame.

Representative example: for a space curve \(\mathbf r(t)\), compute \(\mathbf v(t)\), \(\mathbf a(t)\), speed, and curvature at a specified time.

4. Functions of Several Variables and the Gradient

Students move from curves to surfaces. Contour maps, partial derivatives, tangent planes, gradients, directional derivatives, local extrema, and constrained optimization give a language for how functions change in multiple directions.

Objectives

  • Read contour maps and surfaces.
  • Compute partial derivatives and tangent planes.
  • Use gradients, directional derivatives, and Lagrange multipliers.

Cycles

Cycles 5-7 cover functions of several variables, the gradient, directional derivatives, extrema, and constrained optimization.

Representative example: maximize \(f(x,y)\) subject to \(g(x,y)=c\) by solving \(\nabla f=\lambda \nabla g\).

5. Double Integrals, Triple Integrals, and Change of Variables

Multiple integrals extend accumulation to regions, solids, and densities. Students compute volume, mass, center of mass, polar integrals, triple integrals, and change of variables with Jacobians.

Objectives

  • Set up double and triple integrals over geometric regions.
  • Use polar coordinates and Jacobians.
  • Compute mass and center of mass from density.

Cycles

Cycles 7-10 include double integrals, Fubini's theorem, triple integrals, general regions, polar coordinates, and change of variables.

Representative example: convert a circular-region double integral to polar coordinates and identify the area-scaling factor \(r\).

6. Vector Fields, Line Integrals, Flux, and Theorems

The final arc unifies geometry, differentiation, and integration. Students study vector fields, work, flux, conservative fields, Green's theorem, surface parameterizations, Stokes' theorem, and the Divergence theorem.

Objectives

  • Compute line integrals for work and flux.
  • Use curl and divergence to interpret vector fields.
  • Apply Green's, Stokes', and Divergence theorems.

Cycles

Cycles 9-12 cover line integrals, conservative fields, Green's theorem, surface integrals, flux, Stokes' theorem, and the Divergence theorem.

Representative example: decide whether a vector field is conservative, then compute work either by a line integral or by a potential function.
