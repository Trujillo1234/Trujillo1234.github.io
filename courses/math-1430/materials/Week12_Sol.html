Problem 1
$$ \int \frac{1}{1+4x^2} \, dx $$
Completing the square:

$f(x) = 4x^2 + 1$
$A = 4$
$h = \frac{-B}{2A} = \frac{0}{2(4)} = 0$
$k = f(0) = 4(0)^2 + 1 = 1$

$f(x) = 4(x-0)^2 + 1$
Trigonometric Substitution:

Since $A$ and $k$ have the same sign (both positive), we use tangent.

$x - 0 = \sqrt{\frac{1}{4}} \tan(\theta)$
$x = \frac{1}{2}\tan(\theta)$
$dx = \frac{1}{2}\sec^2(\theta) \, d\theta$
$2x = \tan(\theta) \implies \theta = \tan^{-1}(2x)$
$$ \int \frac{1}{1+4x^2} \, dx $$ $$ = \int \frac{1}{1+4\left(\frac{1}{2}\tan(\theta)\right)^2} \, \frac{1}{2}\sec^2(\theta) \, d\theta $$ $$ = \int \frac{1}{1+\tan^2(\theta)} \, \frac{1}{2}\sec^2(\theta) \, d\theta $$ $$ = \frac{1}{2} \int \frac{1}{\sec^2(\theta)} \sec^2(\theta) \, d\theta $$ $$ = \frac{1}{2} \int \, d\theta $$ $$ = \frac{1}{2}\theta + C $$
$$ = \frac{1}{2}\tan^{-1}(2x) + C $$

Problem 21
Let $R$ be the region bounded above by the semicircle $y=\sqrt{9-x^2}$ and below by the $x$-axis over the interval $[-3,3]$.
a) Find the area of the region.
b) Compute the coordinates of the centroid $(\bar{x},\bar{y})$.

(a) Area

Completing the square:

$f(x) = -x^2 + 9$
$A = -1$
$h = \frac{-B}{2A} = \frac{0}{2(-1)} = 0$
$k = f(0) = -(0)^2 + 9 = 9$

$f(x) = -(x-0)^2 + 9$
Trigonometric Substitution:

Since $A$ is negative and $k$ is positive, we use sine.

$x - 0 = \sqrt{\frac{9}{1}} \sin(\theta)$
$x = 3\sin(\theta)$
$dx = 3\cos(\theta) \, d\theta$
$\frac{x}{3} = \sin(\theta) \implies \theta = \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{x}{3}\right)$

First, find the antiderivative:

$$ \int \sqrt{9-x^2} \, dx $$ $$ = \int \sqrt{9-9\sin^2(\theta)} 3\cos(\theta) \, d\theta $$ $$ = \int \sqrt{9\cos^2(\theta)} 3\cos(\theta) \, d\theta $$ $$ = \int 3\cos(\theta) 3\cos(\theta) \, d\theta $$ $$ = 9 \int \cos^2(\theta) \, d\theta $$ $$ = 9 \int \frac{1}{2}(1+\cos(2\theta)) \, d\theta $$ $$ = \frac{9}{2} \left( \theta + \frac{\sin(2\theta)}{2} \right) + C $$ $$ = \frac{9}{2} ( \theta + \sin(\theta)\cos(\theta) ) + C $$
Draw Triangle
$\frac{x}{3} = \sin(\theta) = \frac{\text{opposite}}{\text{hypotenuse}}$
$\theta$
$\sqrt{9-x^2}$
$x$
$3$
$\cos(\theta) = \frac{\text{adjacent}}{\text{hypotenuse}} = \frac{\sqrt{9-x^2}}{3}$
$$ = \frac{9}{2} \left( \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{x}{3}\right) + \frac{x}{3} \cdot \frac{\sqrt{9-x^2}}{3} \right) + C $$

Evaluating the Definite Integral:

$$ \text{Area} = \left. \frac{9}{2} \left( \sin^{-1}\left(\frac{x}{3}\right) + \frac{x\sqrt{9-x^2}}{9} \right) \right|_{-3}^3 $$ $$ = \frac{9}{2} ( \sin^{-1}(1) - 0 ) - \frac{9}{2} ( \sin^{-1}(-1) - 0 ) $$ $$ = \frac{9}{2} \left( \frac{\pi}{2} \right) - \frac{9}{2} \left( -\frac{\pi}{2} \right) $$ $$ = \frac{9\pi}{4} + \frac{9\pi}{4} = \frac{18\pi}{4} = \frac{9\pi}{2} $$

For $\bar{x}$:

$$ \int_{-3}^3 x\sqrt{9-x^2} \, dx $$
Substitution:
$u = 9-x^2$
$du = -2x \, dx \implies -\frac{1}{2}du = x \, dx$
$u(-3) = 9-(-3)^2 = 0$
$u(3) = 9-(3)^2 = 0$
$$ = -\frac{1}{2} \int_0^0 \sqrt{u} \, du $$ $$ = -\frac{1}{2} \left. \frac{u^{3/2}}{3/2} \right|_0^0 = 0 $$ $$ \bar{x} = \frac{0}{9\pi/2} = 0 $$

For $\bar{y}$:

$$ \int_{-3}^3 \frac{(\sqrt{9-x^2})^2}{2} \, dx $$ $$ = \frac{1}{2} \int_{-3}^3 (9-x^2) \, dx $$ $$ = \left. \frac{1}{2} \left( 9x - \frac{x^3}{3} \right) \right|_{-3}^3 $$ $$ = \frac{1}{2} \left( 9(3) - \frac{(3)^3}{3} \right) - \frac{1}{2} \left( 9(-3) - \frac{(-3)^3}{3} \right) $$ $$ = \frac{1}{2} (27 - 9) - \frac{1}{2} (-27 + 9) $$ $$ = \frac{1}{2}(18) - \frac{1}{2}(-18) $$ $$ = 9 - (-9) = 18 $$ $$ \bar{y} = \frac{18}{9\pi/2} = 18 \cdot \frac{2}{9\pi} = \frac{4}{\pi} $$

$A = 1, B = 4$
$h = \frac{-B}{2A} = \frac{-4}{2(1)} = -2$

$f(x) = (x-(-2))^2 + 1 = (x+2)^2 + 1$

Since $A$ and $k$ have the same sign (both positive), we use tangent.

$x + 2 = \tan(\theta)$
