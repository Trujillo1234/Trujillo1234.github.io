MATH 1430: Calculus II

A content map for the Spring 2026 Calculus II course. The archive is organized around the mathematical arc of the class: series and convergence, power series, integration, applications of integration, and integration techniques.

All Courses Weekly Source Archive

1. Taylor Polynomials and Taylor Series

Students begin by using polynomials as local models for functions. Taylor polynomials make approximation visible, while Taylor series ask when an infinite polynomial genuinely represents a function.

Objectives

  • Construct Taylor polynomials from derivative data.
  • Represent familiar functions with Taylor series.
  • Explain approximation error and local behavior.

Source Notes

Taylor polynomials, Taylor series, and power series.

Representative example: use the first four nonzero terms of the Taylor series for \(e^x\) to approximate \(e^{0.2}\), then explain why the approximation is reasonable near \(x=0\).

2. Convergence Tests

The course then turns from approximation to decision-making: given an infinite series, students learn to choose a test, justify the choice, and distinguish convergence, divergence, absolute convergence, and conditional convergence.

Objectives

  • Use the divergence, geometric, \(p\)-series, alternating, direct comparison, and limit comparison tests.
  • Recognize when absolute convergence implies convergence.
  • Explain why a test applies rather than only naming it.

Source Notes

Divergence test, alternating series, comparison tests, and absolute convergence.

Representative example: compare \(\sum 1/(n^2+n)\) to \(\sum 1/n^2\), then state exactly what the comparison test proves.

3. Power Series and Intervals of Convergence

Power series connect series tests back to functions. Students find radii and intervals of convergence, check endpoints separately, and use series as tools for defining new functions.

Objectives

  • Apply the ratio or root test to find a radius of convergence.
  • Check endpoint behavior with appropriate series tests.
  • Manipulate known series by substitution, differentiation, and integration.

Source Notes

Radius of convergence, intervals, endpoints and functions, and interval of convergence review.

Representative example: find the interval of convergence for \(\sum (x-2)^n/n\), then explain why the endpoints require separate tests.

4. Integral Foundations

The middle of the course rebuilds integration from antiderivatives, definite integrals, net change, and substitution. Students learn to interpret an integral as accumulation, signed change, and geometric area.

Objectives

  • Compute indefinite and definite integrals.
  • Use substitution to reverse the chain rule.
  • Apply the net change theorem in context.

Source Notes

Indefinite integrals and substitution, definite integrals, and net change.

Representative example: evaluate \(\int 2x\sqrt{x^2+1}\,dx\) by choosing \(u=x^2+1\), then interpret the substitution as simplifying a nested rate of change.

5. Applications of Integration

Integration becomes a modeling tool: area, volume, arc length, surface area, work, pumping, center of mass, and centroids are all treated as accumulation problems with a geometric or physical meaning.

Objectives

  • Set up area and volume integrals from geometric slices.
  • Use integrals to compute work, pumping, arc length, and surface area.
  • Compute mass, moments, centers of mass, and centroids.

Source Notes

Area and volume, arc length and surface area, work and energy, pumping, and center of mass.

Representative example: set up the work integral for pumping water out of a tank by describing the weight of a thin slice and the distance it must move.

6. Integration Techniques

The final part of the course is about choosing methods intelligently. Integration by parts, trigonometric integrals, trigonometric substitution, rational functions, and partial fractions are organized around structure rather than memorized pattern matching.

Objectives

  • Use integration by parts for products of functions.
  • Apply trigonometric identities and trigonometric substitution.
  • Decompose rational functions with partial fractions.

Source Notes

Integration by parts, trigonometric integrals, trigonometric substitution, and rational functions.

Representative example: decide whether \(\int x e^x\,dx\), \(\int \sqrt{9-x^2}\,dx\), or \(\int 1/(x^2-1)\,dx\) calls for parts, trigonometric substitution, or partial fractions.
