Commitments

  • Reasoning before answer-getting.
  • Steps that can be checked by another reader.
  • Definitions treated as load-bearing ideas.
  • Revision as ordinary mathematical practice.
  • Courses with visible structure.

Philosophy

I want students to experience mathematics as a form of communication. A correct answer matters, but it is rarely the whole mathematical act. Students also need to say what their symbols mean, show why each step is allowed, and write in a way that lets another person check the work without guessing what happened in their head.

This is why I put so much emphasis on reasoning and visible structure. In my courses, "show your work" is not a decorative rule. It means: state the objects, identify the assumptions, preserve the relevant quantities, justify the algebra, and leave a path that someone else can follow. I want students to build habits of public reason: claims should be intelligible, checkable, and connected to the definitions and theorems that support them.

I do not think mathematical confidence comes from lowering the intellectual level of a course. It comes from making the route through a hard subject visible. I use backward design, active learning, and constructive alignment so that notes, examples, classwork, homework, quizzes, exams, and feedback all point toward the same habits of mind.

Course Design

I design courses as coherent learning systems rather than as isolated lectures. Students should be able to see how today's work connects to yesterday's examples, next week's assessment, and the larger mathematical object the course is building toward. In calculus, differential equations, and upper-division courses, this often means explicit learning objectives, recurring examples, guided classwork, public solution standards, and review that asks students to name the idea behind the procedure.

A definition is not a vocabulary word on the way to the "real" problem. It is often the load-bearing structure of the entire section. I try to slow down at those moments: domain restrictions, quantifiers, equivalence relations, convergence, linearity, independence, and function-as-object all require students to reorganize what they think counts as an explanation.

Assessment and Feedback

Assessment should tell students what kind of mathematical work the course values. Homework and classwork are places to practice the form of reasoning; quizzes and exams are places to show that reasoning with more independence. I use formative feedback, office-hour conversations, solution keys, and revision opportunities to help students diagnose where an argument or computation stops being checkable.

In proof-based courses, I have used standards-based grading so that students can see the particular objectives they are building toward. In computational courses, I still want more than templates: students should know what a method assumes, what it preserves, and how to recognize when an answer is impossible.

Student Memory

I enjoy hearing comments from students who remember a course not only because it was organized, but because the organization helped them become more independent. Those are the comments that matter most to me: students remembering that the notes, classwork, homework, quizzes, and exams fit together; students remembering that their written work became clearer; students remembering that a difficult subject became something they could enter and discuss.

Mentoring

Mentoring extends the same philosophy beyond a single course. In independent studies, undergraduate research, and capstone work, students learn to produce mathematical artifacts: notes, examples, conjectures, computations, diagrams, proofs, talks, and drafts. The goal is not only skill acquisition. It is a shift from watching mathematics happen to participating in it responsibly.
