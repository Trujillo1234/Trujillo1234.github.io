The Indefinite Integral

Learning Objectives
  • Understand the indefinite integral and its relation to antiderivatives.
  • Memorize and apply foundational indefinite integral rules.
  • Evaluate complex integrals using the Change of Variables (U-Substitution) method.

1. The Indefinite Integral

When $F'(x) = f(x)$ we write:

$$ \int f(x) \, dx = F(x) + C $$

and say that $F(x)$ is an antiderivative of $f(x)$.

Examples Memorize these!

$$ \begin{align*} (\sin(x))' &= \cos(x) & &\longrightarrow & \int \cos(x) \, dx &= \sin(x) + C \\[8pt] (-\cos(x))' &= \sin(x) & &\longrightarrow & \int \sin(x) \, dx &= -\cos(x) + C \\[8pt] (e^x)' &= e^x & &\longrightarrow & \int e^x \, dx &= e^x + C \\[8pt] (\ln|x|)' &= \frac{1}{x} & &\longrightarrow & \int \frac{1}{x} \, dx &= \ln|x| + C \\[8pt] \left(\frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1}\right)' &= \frac{(n+1)x^{n+1-1}}{n+1} = x^n \quad (n \neq -1) & &\longrightarrow & \int x^n \, dx &= \frac{x^{n+1}}{n+1} + C \end{align*} $$

Why the constant?

Theorem

If $F'(x) = G'(x)$ then there is a constant $C$ such that $F(x) = G(x) + C$.

Proof:
Suppose $F'(x) = G'(x)$.
Then, $F'(x) - G'(x) = 0$.
This implies that $(F(x) - G(x))' = 0$.
Therefore, $F(x) - G(x)$ is constant, i.e., $$ F(x) - G(x) = C $$ for some constant $C$. $\blacksquare$

2. Change of Variables (u-sub.)

Suppose that $\displaystyle \int f(x) \, dx = F(x) + C$.

Then $\displaystyle \int f(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) \, dx = F(g(x)) + C$.

Why? (Chain Rule)
Note that $F'(x) = f(x)$.
$$ (F(g(x)))' = F'(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) = f(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) $$ That is, in integral form: $$ \int f(g(x))g'(x) \, dx = F(g(x)) + C $$

In practice we let $u = g(x)$ and think of $u$ as a new auxiliary variable.

$$ u = g(x) $$ $$ du = g'(x) \, dx $$ $$ \int f(g(x)) \cdot g'(x) \, dx = \int f(u) \, du = F(u) + C = F(g(x)) + C $$

3. U-Substitution Examples

Example 1: $\displaystyle \int \frac{1}{x \ln(x)} \, dx$
Rewrite the integral to group the derivative parts: $$ \int \frac{1}{\ln(x)} \cdot \frac{1}{x} \, dx $$ Let $u = \ln(x)$, then $du = \frac{1}{x} \, dx$. $$ = \int \frac{1}{u} \, du = \ln|u| + C $$ Substitute $u$ back in: $$ = \ln|\ln(x)| + C $$
Example 2: $\displaystyle \int \frac{(\ln(x))^5}{x} \, dx$
Rewrite the integral: $$ \int (\ln(x))^5 \cdot \frac{1}{x} \, dx $$ Let $u = \ln(x)$, then $du = \frac{1}{x} \, dx$. $$ = \int u^5 \, du = \frac{u^6}{6} + C $$ Substitute $u$ back in: $$ = \frac{(\ln(x))^6}{6} + C $$
Example 3: $\displaystyle \int \sin(x)\cos(x) \, dx$
Let $u = \sin(x)$, then $du = \cos(x) \, dx$. $$ = \int u \, du = \frac{u^2}{2} + C $$ Substitute $u$ back in: $$ = \frac{\sin^2(x)}{2} + C $$
Check Answer:
$$ \left(\frac{\sin^2(x)}{2}\right)' = \frac{2(\sin(x))^{2-1}}{2} \cdot \cos(x) = \sin(x)\cos(x) $$
Example 4: $\displaystyle \int x \sin(x^2) \, dx$
Rewrite the integral: $$ \int \sin(x^2) \cdot x \, dx $$ Let $u = x^2$, then $du = 2x \, dx$, which means $\frac{1}{2}du = x \, dx$. $$ = \int \sin(u) \cdot \frac{1}{2} \, du = \frac{1}{2} \int \sin(u) \, du $$ $$ = -\frac{1}{2}\cos(u) + C = -\frac{1}{2}\cos(x^2) + C $$
Example 5: $\displaystyle \int \tan(x) \, dx$
Rewrite using sine and cosine: $$ \int \frac{\sin(x)}{\cos(x)} \, dx = \int \frac{1}{\cos(x)} \cdot \sin(x) \, dx $$ Let $u = \cos(x)$, then $du = -\sin(x) \, dx$, which means $-du = \sin(x) \, dx$. $$ = \int \frac{1}{u} \cdot (-du) = -\int \frac{1}{u} \, du $$ $$ = -\ln|u| + C = -\ln|\cos(x)| + C $$ Using log properties, this becomes: $$ = \ln|\sec(x)| + C $$
Add to your list:
$$ \int \tan(x) \, dx = \ln|\sec(x)| + C $$
Example 6: $\displaystyle \int \frac{1}{2x+5} \, dx$
Let $u = 2x+5$, then $du = 2 \, dx$, which means $\frac{1}{2}du = dx$. $$ = \int \frac{1}{u} \cdot \frac{1}{2} \, du = \frac{1}{2} \int \frac{1}{u} \, du $$ $$ = \frac{1}{2}\ln|u| + C = \frac{1}{2}\ln|2x+5| + C $$
Check your work:
$$ \left(\frac{1}{2}\ln|2x+5|\right)' = \frac{1}{2} \cdot \frac{2}{2x+5} = \frac{1}{2x+5} $$
