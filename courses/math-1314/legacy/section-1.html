Section 1 Introduction to Precalculus

Section Context

The opening section explains how the course is organized and how students should use the archive. It frames precalculus as a set of habits: practice, feedback, representation, and steady movement between algebraic and graphical thinking.

  • Learning objectives: understand course expectations, identify the main course outcomes, and use the table of contents, videos, homework, and sample exams as a study system.
  • Representative example: take one course objective, such as interpreting a graph, and connect it to a video, a homework problem, and an exam-style question.
  • Connection forward: the rest of the course repeatedly asks students to translate among words, symbols, graphs, and numerical evidence.

Subsection 1.1 A Growth Mindset

Your brain is a like a muscle. With sustained practice your brain's neural network

  • grows new connections,

  • reinforces existing connections, and

  • increases the transmission speeds between neurons.

Students with a growth mindset understand these important neural facts by reconginzing that intelligence can be developed.

Subsection 1.2 Course Description

Topics include a brief review of introductory algebra, variation, elementary theory of equations, functions (including exponential and logarithmic) inequalities, systems of equations, and other related topics.

Remark 1.2.
The primary objective of this course is to prepare students for success in the sciences, including the Health Sciences.

Precalculus Algebra covers the fundamental mathematical calculations required of working with a single unknown, the skills necessary throughout the sciences. Specifically, this course is focused on the definitions, properties, and applications of the elementary functions from algebra that are used in the sciences including the polynomial, rational, power, exponential, and logarithmic functions.

Subsection 1.3 Course Learning Outcomes

Students successfully completing this course will be able to:

  • Solve linear and quadratic equations and display correct use of algebraic notation, including correct use of the equal sign.

  • Use function notation and demonstrate understanding of functions as a fundamental mathematical concept.

  • Demonstrate understanding of function composition by correctly computing the composition of various functions.

  • Demonstrate competence with one-to-one functions and when appropriate, compute the inverse of a given function.

  • Demonstrate the geometrical view of functions by using simple graphing techniques to draw the shape of various elementary functions.

  • Solve equations involving polynomial and rational functions.

  • Solve equations involving exponential functions and use those functions to solve problems in exponential growth.

  • Solve equations involving logarithmic functions and demonstrate understanding of the logarithmic function as an inverse function.

  • Demonstrate an understanding of mathematical modeling by using algebra to solve applied problems.

Subsection 1.4 Midterm and Final

All 1314 students are required to take the departmental midterm and final exams. Each exam is worth 25% of the student's total grade. Exams will include problems that are similar to homework/quiz problems and the examples that are worked in class. Each exam will consist of problems where students must:

  • communicate all of the work correctly,

  • demonstrate how they arrived at the correct solution to the problem.

Emphasis is on correct communication of the work. A correct answer without supporting work will usually be graded as incomplete or incorrect. questions.

Section 7 includes a practice midterm exam and practice final exam with video solutions.

Subsection 1.5 Edfinity Homework

https://edfinity.com/about/company

Edfinity is a low-cost WeBWorK-compatible homework and assessment system in use at over 200 institutions and supported by the National Science Foundation.

There will be a total of 36 different homework assignments over the course of the semester. Each week 2-3 assignments will be assigned by your instructor. A typical assignment takes between 2.5 and 3 hours of time to complete.

Remark 1.3.
Each week you will need to allocate between 5 and 9 hours of time to complete your Edfinity homework assignments.

During the first week of class your instructor will provide you with information about how to enroll in the Edfinity class. Please wait until the first day of class to sign-up for your account since the refund period is two-weeks. If you decide to drop the course within the first two-weeks then you can get a full refund for the Edfinity software.

Subsection 1.6 Calculator Policy

Only a scientific calculator is allowed on exams. A graphing calculator may NOT be used on the midterm or final exams.

You are encouraged to use graphing calculator to complete your homework and explore the graphs of relations and functions. However please recognize that a graphing calculator is not allowed on any exams.

Desmos is a high-quality online graphing calculator that can be used for free. https://www.desmos.com

Subsection 1.7 Contact Hours

The state of Texas requires that in a 3-credit hour course students spend 2.5 hours per week in the classroom in direct contact with their instructor. Students are expected to work outside of the classroom without the supervision of your instructor applying course material a minimum of 7.5 hours per week.

Remark 1.4.
Students earning a passing grade should spend on average a minimum of 10 hours per week to complete the course.
