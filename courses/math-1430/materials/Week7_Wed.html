1. The Definite Integral & Riemann Sums

The definite integral is a fundamental concept in calculus that generalizes the calculation of area.

The definite integral represents the signed area between the curve $y = f(x)$ and the x-axis from $x=a$ to $x=b$.
Area above the x-axis contributes positively, while area below contributes negatively.
$$ \int_a^b f(x) \, dx = \begin{matrix} \text{signed area between} \\ \text{the curve and the x-axis.} \end{matrix} $$

Formal Definition

The definite integral is formally defined as the limit of a Riemann sum as the number of subintervals $n$ approaches infinity:

$$ \int_a^b f(x) \, dx = \lim_{n \to \infty} \Big( f(x_1) + f(x_2) + \dots + f(x_n) \Big) \Delta x $$ $$ = \lim_{n \to \infty} \sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i) \Delta x $$
where   $\displaystyle \Delta x = \frac{b-a}{n}$   and   $x_i = a + i\Delta x$

Riemann Sums (Visualizing the Definition)

To understand the definition above, imagine approximating the area under the curve using $n$ rectangles. The width of each rectangle is $\Delta x$, and the height is determined by the function value $f(x_i)$.

Approximation with $n=3$
Approximation with $n=9$

For $n=3$ rectangles:

$$ \sum_{i=1}^3 f(x_i)\Delta x = \underbrace{f(x_1)\Delta x}_{A_1} + \underbrace{f(x_2)\Delta x}_{A_2} + \underbrace{f(x_3)\Delta x}_{A_3} $$

For $n$ rectangles:

$$ \sum_{i=1}^n f(x_i)\Delta x = A_1 + A_2 + A_3 + \dots + A_n $$

$\vdots$
Keep going!! Let $n \to \infty$

As the number of rectangles approaches infinity, the width $\Delta x$ shrinks to $dx$, and the sum exactly matches the area under the curve.

2. The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus bridges the gap between differential and integral calculus. It tells us that we can evaluate a definite integral (which represents an area) simply by using an antiderivative.

The Fundamental Theorem of Calculus

If $\displaystyle \int f(x) \, dx = F(x) + C$   then

$\displaystyle \int_a^b f(x) \, dx = F(b) - F(a)$

Proof Outline

Step 1: Define an "Area Accumulation" Function

Let $\displaystyle A(x) = \int_a^x f(t) \, dt$. This function gives the area under the curve from a fixed starting point $a$ up to a variable point $x$.

$A(x)$ is the total area from $a$ to $x$.
A small change in area: $A(x+h) - A(x)$.

Step 2: Find the Derivative of the Area Function

If we advance $x$ by a tiny width $h$, the extra area added is $A(x+h) - A(x)$. As seen in the right diagram, this sliver is approximately a rectangle with width $h$ and height $f(x)$:

$$ A(x+h) - A(x) \approx f(x) \cdot h $$

Dividing by $h$ and taking the limit as $h \to 0$ yields the exact derivative of our area function:

$$ A'(x) = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{A(x+h) - A(x)}{h} = \lim_{h \to 0} \frac{f(x) \cdot h}{h} = f(x) $$

Step 3: Relate to the Antiderivative

Therefore, $F'(x) = f(x) = A'(x)$. Since $A(x)$ and $F(x)$ have the same derivative, they must be the same function up to some constant $C$:

$$ A(x) = F(x) + C $$

To find this constant $C$, we evaluate the area at the starting point $x=a$. The area from $a$ to $a$ is zero:

$$ \int_a^a f(x) \, dx = 0 \implies 0 = A(a) = F(a) + C $$

This tells us that $C = -F(a)$. Substituting this back into our equation gives $A(x) = F(x) - F(a)$.

Step 4: Conclude for the Bounds $a$ to $b$

Finally, the total definite integral from $a$ to $b$ is exactly the area function evaluated at $b$:

$$ \int_a^b f(t) \, dt = A(b) = F(b) - F(a). \quad \blacksquare $$

3. Evaluating Definite Integrals

Example 1: $\displaystyle \int_0^\pi \sin(x) \, dx$
$$ \int_0^\pi \sin(x) \, dx = -\cos(x) \Big|_0^\pi $$ $$ = -\cos(\pi) + \cos(0) $$ $$ = -(-1) + 1 = 2 $$
Example 2: $\displaystyle \int_0^{\pi/2} \sin(x)\cos(x) \, dx$
Change of Variables (Update Bounds!):
$u = \sin(x)$
$du = \cos(x) \, dx$
$u(0) = \sin(0) = 0$
$u(\pi/2) = \sin(\pi/2) = 1$
$$ = \int_{u(0)}^{u(\pi/2)} u \, du = \int_0^1 u \, du $$ $$ = \frac{u^2}{2} \Big|_0^1 = \frac{1}{2} - 0 = \frac{1}{2} $$
Example 3: $\displaystyle \int_1^2 \frac{\ln(x)}{x} \, dx$
Change of Variables:
$u = \ln(x)$
$du = \frac{1}{x} \, dx$
$u(1) = \ln(1) = 0$
$u(2) = \ln(2)$
$$ \int_1^2 \ln(x) \cdot \frac{1}{x} \, dx = \int_{u(1)}^{u(2)} u \, du = \int_0^{\ln(2)} u \, du $$ $$ = \frac{u^2}{2} \Big|_0^{\ln(2)} = \frac{(\ln(2))^2}{2} - \frac{0^2}{2} = \frac{(\ln(2))^2}{2} $$
Example 4: $\displaystyle \int_0^{\pi/4} \tan(x) \, dx$
$$ = \ln|\sec(x)| \Big|_0^{\pi/4} $$ $$ = \ln|\sec(\pi/4)| - \ln|\sec(0)| $$ $$ = \ln(\sqrt{2}) - \ln(1) $$ $$ = \ln(\sqrt{2}) - 0 = \ln(\sqrt{2}) $$

Some Properties of Integrals

$$ \int k f(x) \, dx = k \int f(x) \, dx $$ $$ \int f(x) \pm g(x) \, dx = \int f(x) \, dx \pm \int g(x) \, dx $$
Example 5: $\displaystyle \int_0^{\pi/4} 25 \tan(x) \, dx$
$$ = 25 \ln|\sec(x)| \Big|_0^{\pi/4} $$ $$ = 25 \ln|\sec(\pi/4)| - 25 \ln|\sec(0)| $$ $$ = 25 \cdot \ln(\sqrt{2}) $$
Example 6: $\displaystyle \int_2^3 \frac{1}{5x+1} \, dx$
Change of Variables:
$u = 5x+1$
$du = 5 \, dx \implies \frac{1}{5}du = dx$
$u(2) = 5(2)+1 = 11$
$u(3) = 5(3)+1 = 16$
$$ = \int_{u(2)}^{u(3)} \frac{1}{u} \cdot \frac{1}{5} \, du = \frac{1}{5} \int_{11}^{16} \frac{1}{u} \, du $$ $$ = \frac{1}{5} \ln|u| \Big|_{11}^{16} = \frac{1}{5}\ln|16| - \frac{1}{5}\ln|11| $$
Example 7: $\displaystyle \int_0^3 e^{2x} \, dx$
Change of Variables:
$u = 2x$
$du = 2 \, dx \implies \frac{1}{2}du = dx$
$u(0) = 2(0) = 0$
$u(3) = 2(3) = 6$
$$ = \int_0^6 e^u \cdot \frac{1}{2} \, du = \frac{1}{2} \int_0^6 e^u \, du $$ $$ = \frac{1}{2} e^u \Big|_0^6 = \frac{1}{2}e^6 - \frac{1}{2}e^0 $$ $$ = \frac{1}{2}e^6 - \frac{1}{2} $$
