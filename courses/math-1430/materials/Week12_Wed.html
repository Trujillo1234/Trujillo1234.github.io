Trigonometric Integrals

Calculus 2 • Wednesday Notes
Key Learning Objectives

Finish remaining trigonometric cases and evaluate integrals of the form: $$ \int \sec^n(x) \tan^m(x) \, dx $$ where $n$ and $m$ are non-negative integers.

1. Finishing Powers of Sine and Cosine

Recap: Evaluating $\displaystyle \int \sin^n(x) \cos^m(x) \, dx$

Case 1
(Power of cosine is odd)

  1. Keep one $\cos(x)$ next to the $dx$.
  2. Convert all other cosines to sines using the Pythagorean identity:
    $\cos^2(x) = 1 - \sin^2(x)$
  3. Make the substitution:
    $u = \sin(x)$

Case 2
(Power of sine is odd)

  1. Keep one $\sin(x)$ next to the $dx$.
  2. Convert all other sines to cosines using the Pythagorean identity:
    $\sin^2(x) = 1 - \cos^2(x)$
  3. Make the substitution:
    $u = \cos(x)$

Case 3 ($n$ and $m$ are both even)

Use trigonometric identities (Power Reducing Formulas) to lower the degree of the expression:

$$ \sin^2(x) = \frac{1}{2}(1 - \cos(2x)) $$
$$ \cos^2(x) = \frac{1}{2}(1 + \cos(2x)) $$
Example
$$ \int \sin^2(x) \, dx = \int \frac{1}{2}(1 - \cos(2x)) \, dx $$ $$ = \frac{1}{2}\left(x - \frac{\sin(2x)}{2}\right) + C $$
$$ = \frac{x}{2} - \frac{\sin(2x)}{4} + C $$

2. Powers of Secant and Tangent

Important Facts / Recall

$$ (\tan(x))' = \sec^2(x) $$ $$ (\sec(x))' = \sec(x)\tan(x) $$ $$ \int \sec^2(x) \, dx = \tan(x) + C $$ $$ \int \tan(x) \, dx = \ln|\sec(x)| + C $$

Historical Context

$$ \int \sec(x) \, dx = \, ?? $$

  • Mercator Map (1569): To map rhumb lines as straight lines, cartographers needed the area under $\sec(x)$ to construct accurate maps.
  • Edward Wright (1599): Evaluated the integrals by numerical methods, i.e., Riemann sums: $$ \int_a^b \sec(x) \, dx \approx \sum_{i=1}^n \sec(x_i) \Delta x \quad \text{where } \Delta x = \frac{b-a}{n}, \, x_1 = a + \Delta x, \, \dots, \, x_n = b $$ Wright asked, "Is there a formula?"
  • Henry Bond (1640): Conjectured from logarithm tables that $\int_0^{\phi} \sec(x) \, dx = \ln|\sec(\phi) + \tan(\phi)|$ in his book "Epitome of Navigation".
  • James Gregory (1668): Provided the first proof that $\int \sec(x) \, dx = \ln|\sec(x) + \tan(x)| + C$, which was also the first use of partial fractions.
Why? (Proof by differentiation)
$$ \left( \ln|\sec(x) + \tan(x)| \right)' = \frac{\sec(x)\tan(x) + \sec^2(x)}{\sec(x) + \tan(x)} $$ $$ = \frac{\sec(x)(\tan(x) + \sec(x))}{\sec(x) + \tan(x)} = \sec(x) $$
Thus,  
$$ \int \sec(x) \, dx = \ln|\sec(x) + \tan(x)| + C $$
One more for the list:
$$ I = \int \sec^3(x) \, dx = \int \sec(x)\sec^2(x) \, dx $$
$u = \sec(x)$ $\longrightarrow$ $du = \sec(x)\tan(x) \, dx$
$dv = \sec^2(x) \, dx$ $\longrightarrow$ $v = \tan(x)$
$$ I = \sec(x)\tan(x) - \int \sec(x)\tan^2(x) \, dx $$
$\tan^2(x) = \sec^2(x) - 1$

$$ I = \sec(x)\tan(x) - \int \sec(x)(\sec^2(x) - 1) \, dx $$ $$ I = \sec(x)\tan(x) - \int \sec^3(x) - \sec(x) \, dx $$ $$ I = \sec(x)\tan(x) - I + \ln|\sec(x) + \tan(x)| $$
$$ I = \frac{1}{2}\left( \sec(x)\tan(x) + \ln|\sec(x) + \tan(x)| \right) + C $$

Memorize This List for Final Exam

$$ \left\{ \begin{aligned} \int \sec(x) \, dx &= \ln|\sec(x) + \tan(x)| + C \\[1.5ex] \int \sec^2(x) \, dx &= \tan(x) + C \\[1.5ex] \int \sec^3(x) \, dx &= \frac{1}{2}\left( \sec(x)\tan(x) + \ln|\sec(x) + \tan(x)| \right) + C \\[1.5ex] \int \tan(x) \, dx &= \ln|\sec(x)| + C \end{aligned} \right. $$

3. Strategies for $\int \sec^n(x)\tan^m(x) \, dx$

Case 1 (The power of $\sec(x)$ is even, $n$ is even)

  1. Move two secants ($\sec^2(x)$) next to the $dx$: $$ \int \sec^{n-2}(x)\tan^m(x) \sec^2(x) \, dx $$
  2. Rewrite $\sec^{n-2}(x)$ in terms of $\tan(x)$ using: $$ \sec^2(x) = \tan^2(x) + 1 $$
  3. Make the $u$-sub $u = \tan(x)$, $du = \sec^2(x) \, dx$ to find the antiderivative.
Example
$$ \int \sec^4(x)\tan^4(x) \, dx = \int \sec^2(x)\tan^4(x)\sec^2(x) \, dx $$ $$ = \int (\tan^2(x) + 1)\tan^4(x)\sec^2(x) \, dx $$
$u = \tan(x)$
$du = \sec^2(x) \, dx$
$$ = \int (u^2 + 1)u^4 \, du $$ $$ = \int (u^6 + u^4) \, du $$ $$ = \frac{u^7}{7} + \frac{u^5}{5} + C $$
$$ = \frac{\tan^7(x)}{7} + \frac{\tan^5(x)}{5} + C $$
Example
$$ \int \sec^6(x)\tan^4(x) \, dx = \int \sec^4(x)\tan^4(x)\sec^2(x) \, dx $$ $$ = \int (1+\tan^2(x))(1+\tan^2(x))\tan^4(x)\sec^2(x) \, dx $$
$u = \tan(x)$
$du = \sec^2(x) \, dx$
$$ = \int (1+u^2)(1+u^2)u^4 \, du $$ $$ = \int (1+2u^2+u^4)u^4 \, du $$ $$ = \int (u^4 + 2u^6 + u^8) \, du $$ $$ = \frac{u^5}{5} + \frac{2u^7}{7} + \frac{u^9}{9} + C $$
$$ = \frac{\tan^5(x)}{5} + \frac{2\tan^7(x)}{7} + \frac{\tan^9(x)}{9} + C $$

Case 2 (Power of tangent is odd, $m$ is odd & $n > 0$)

  1. Move $\sec(x)\tan(x)$ next to $dx$: $$ \int \sec^{n-1}(x)\tan^{m-1}(x) \sec(x)\tan(x) \, dx $$
  2. Rewrite $\tan^{m-1}(x)$ in terms of $\sec(x)$ using: $$ \tan^2(x) = \sec^2(x) - 1 $$
  3. Apply the $u$-sub   ${u = \sec(x)}$, ${du = \sec(x)\tan(x) \, dx}$   to find the antiderivative.
Example
$$ \int \sec^3(x)\tan^3(x) \, dx = \int \sec^2(x)\tan^2(x)\sec(x)\tan(x) \, dx $$ $$ = \int \sec^2(x)(\sec^2(x) - 1)\sec(x)\tan(x) \, dx $$
$u = \sec(x)$
$du = \sec(x)\tan(x) \, dx$
$$ = \int u^2(u^2 - 1) \, du $$ $$ = \int (u^4 - u^2) \, du $$ $$ = \frac{u^5}{5} - \frac{u^3}{3} + C $$
$$ = \frac{\sec^5(x)}{5} - \frac{\sec^3(x)}{3} + C $$
Example
$$ \int \sec^5(x)\tan^3(x) \, dx = \int \sec^4(x)\tan^2(x)\sec(x)\tan(x) \, dx $$ $$ = \int \sec^4(x)(\sec^2(x) - 1)\sec(x)\tan(x) \, dx $$
$u = \sec(x)$
$du = \sec(x)\tan(x) \, dx$
$$ = \int u^4(u^2 - 1) \, du $$ $$ = \int (u^6 - u^4) \, du $$ $$ = \frac{u^7}{7} - \frac{u^5}{5} + C $$
$$ = \frac{\sec^7(x)}{7} - \frac{\sec^5(x)}{5} + C $$

Case 3 (Otherwise)

Power of $\sec(x)$ is odd and power of $\tan(x)$ is even, OR power of secant is zero.

  • Convert everything to secant using $\tan^2(x) = \sec^2(x) - 1$.
  • Use the memorized formulas for $\int \sec(x) \, dx$, $\int \sec^2(x) \, dx$, $\int \sec^3(x) \, dx$.
  • Use integration by parts for higher powers.
Example
$$ \int \sec(x)\tan^2(x) \, dx = \int \sec(x)(\sec^2(x) - 1) \, dx $$ $$ = \int \left( \sec^3(x) - \sec(x) \right) \, dx $$
$$ = \frac{1}{2}\left( \sec(x)\tan(x) + \ln|\sec(x) + \tan(x)| \right) - \ln|\sec(x) + \tan(x)| + C $$
Example
$$ \int \tan^2(x) \, dx = \int (\sec^2(x) - 1) \, dx $$
$$ = \tan(x) - x + C $$

Summary: Evaluating $\displaystyle \int \sec^n(x) \tan^m(x) \, dx$

Case 1
(Power of secant is even)

  1. Move two secants, $\sec^2(x)$, next to the $dx$.
  2. Convert remaining secants to tangents using the identity:
    $\sec^2(x) = \tan^2(x) + 1$
  3. Make the substitution:
    $u = \tan(x)$

Case 2
(Power of tangent is odd & $n > 0$)

  1. Move $\sec(x)\tan(x)$ next to the $dx$.
  2. Convert remaining tangents to secants using the identity:
    $\tan^2(x) = \sec^2(x) - 1$
  3. Make the substitution:
    $u = \sec(x)$

Case 3 (Otherwise)

Applies when the power of $\sec(x)$ is odd and power of $\tan(x)$ is even, OR power of secant is zero.

  • Convert everything to secants using $\tan^2(x) = \sec^2(x) - 1$.
  • Use the memorized formulas for $\int \sec(x) \, dx$, $\int \sec^2(x) \, dx$, and $\int \sec^3(x) \, dx$.
  • Use Integration by Parts for higher powers.
