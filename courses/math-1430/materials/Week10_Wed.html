Center of Mass & Centroids

Calculus 2 • Notes
Learning Objectives
  • Determine the center of mass for discrete point masses in 1D and 2D.
  • Set up and evaluate integrals to find the centroid of a continuous region (Type 1 bounded regions).

1. Discrete Point Masses

Let's first consider two point masses balancing on a 1D line (like a seesaw). We want to find the balance point, $\bar{x}$.

By the principle of moments, the system balances when:

$$ m_1(\bar{x} - x_1) = m_2(x_2 - \bar{x}) $$ $$ m_1\bar{x} - m_1x_1 = m_2x_2 - m_2\bar{x} $$ $$ m_1\bar{x} + m_2\bar{x} = m_1x_1 + m_2x_2 $$ $$ \bar{x}(m_1 + m_2) = m_1x_1 + m_2x_2 $$
$$ \bar{x} = \frac{m_1x_1 + m_2x_2}{m_1 + m_2} $$

We can generalize this. For n-point masses on a line:

$$ \bar{x} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n m_ix_i}{\sum_{i=1}^n m_i} $$

In two-dimensional space, the logic applies to both axes. If we have point masses $m_1, m_2, \dots, m_n$ at points $(x_1, y_1), (x_2, y_2), \dots$

$$ \bar{x} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n m_ix_i}{\sum_{i=1}^n m_i} $$
$$ \bar{y} = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n m_iy_i}{\sum_{i=1}^n m_i} $$

2. Centroid of a Region of Type 1

Consider a continuous region with uniform mass density $\delta$, bounded above by $y = f(x)$ and below by $y = g(x)$ on the interval $[a, b]$. We want to find its center of mass, also known as the centroid.

$i^{\text{th}}$ rectangle

By symmetry, the center of mass of the $i^{\text{th}}$ rectangle is located at its midpoint:

$$ \left( \frac{x_{i-1} + x_i}{2}, \frac{f(x_i) + g(x_i)}{2} \right) $$

The mass $m_i$ of this rectangle is its Area $\times$ Density $\delta$:

$$ m_i = \delta \cdot \big[f(x_i) - g(x_i)\big] \Delta x $$

If we treat each rectangle as a point mass located at its center of mass, we can use the discrete formulas and take the Riemann sum limits as $n \to \infty$. (Notice that the uniform density $\delta$ will cancel out in the numerator and denominator!)

Approximate Coordinates:

$$ \bar{x} \approx \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n \big[f(x_i) - g(x_i)\big] \left( \frac{x_{i-1}+x_i}{2} \right) \Delta x}{\sum_{i=1}^n \big[f(x_i) - g(x_i)\big] \Delta x} $$ $$ \bar{y} \approx \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n \big[f(x_i) - g(x_i)\big] \left( \frac{f(x_i)+g(x_i)}{2} \right) \Delta x}{\sum_{i=1}^n \big[f(x_i) - g(x_i)\big] \Delta x} $$

Centroid Formula (Continuous Region)

Taking the limit as $n \to \infty$, we arrive at the exact integrals for the centroid $(\bar{x}, \bar{y})$:

$$ \bar{x} = \frac{\int_a^b x \big[f(x) - g(x)\big] \,dx}{\int_a^b \big[f(x) - g(x)\big] \,dx} $$
$$ \bar{y} = \frac{\int_a^b \frac{1}{2} \big[f(x)^2 - g(x)^2\big] \,dx}{\int_a^b \big[f(x) - g(x)\big] \,dx} $$

Note: The denominator $\int_a^b \big[f(x) - g(x)\big] \,dx$ is simply the Area of the region!

3. Example Problem

Example:

Find the center of mass of the region bounded by $y = -x^2 + 3x - 2$ and the $x$-axis.

Here, the top curve is $f(x) = -x^2 + 3x - 2$ and the bottom curve is the $x$-axis, so $g(x) = 0$. The roots are $x=1$ and $x=2$.

Step 1: Calculate the Area (Denominator)

$$ \text{Area} = \int_1^2 (-x^2 + 3x - 2) \,dx = \left[ -\frac{x^3}{3} + \frac{3x^2}{2} - 2x \right]_1^2 = \frac{1}{6} $$

Step 2: Calculate $\bar{x}$

$$ \text{Numerator} = \int_1^2 x(-x^2 + 3x - 2) \,dx = \int_1^2 (-x^3 + 3x^2 - 2x) \,dx $$ $$ = \left[ -\frac{x^4}{4} + x^3 - x^2 \right]_1^2 = \frac{1}{4} $$
$$ \bar{x} = \frac{\text{Numerator}}{\text{Area}} = \frac{1/4}{1/6} = \frac{1}{4} \cdot \frac{6}{1} = \frac{6}{4} = 1.5 $$

Step 3: Calculate $\bar{y}$

$$ \text{Numerator} = \int_1^2 \frac{1}{2}(-x^2 + 3x - 2)^2 \,dx $$ $$ = \frac{1}{2} \int_1^2 (x^4 - 6x^3 + 13x^2 - 12x + 4) \,dx $$ $$ = \frac{1}{2} \left[ \frac{x^5}{5} - \frac{3x^4}{2} + \frac{13x^3}{3} - 6x^2 + 4x \right]_1^2 $$

(Finding a common denominator and evaluating...)

$$ = \frac{1}{60} $$
$$ \bar{y} = \frac{\text{Numerator}}{\text{Area}} = \frac{1/60}{1/6} = \frac{1}{60} \cdot \frac{6}{1} = \frac{6}{60} = \frac{1}{10} = 0.1 $$
Centroid: $\mathbf{(1.5, \, 0.1)}$
