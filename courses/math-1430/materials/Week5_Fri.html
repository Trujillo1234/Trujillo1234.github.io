Learning Objectives
  • Use the direct comparison test to show that a series converges or diverges
  • Use the limit comparison test to show that a series converges or diverges
  • Use absolute convergence to show that a series converges.

1. The Comparison Tests

Direct Comparison Test

Assume that there is some $N$ such that for all $n \ge N$: $$ 0 < b_n \le c_n $$
  1. If $\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n$ converges, then $\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} b_n$ also converges.
  2. If $\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} b_n$ diverges, then $\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n$ also diverges.

Limit Comparison Test

Suppose that $b_n>0$ and $c_n>0$ for all $n$.

If the limit $\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{b_n}{c_n} $ exists and $$ 0 < \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{b_n}{c_n} < \infty $$ then the two series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} b_n$ and $\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n$ either both converge or both diverge.

2. Examples

Example 1: Determine if $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{3^n+n^2}$ converges or diverges.
We use the Limit Comparison Test. We compare to the geometric series $\sum \left(\frac{1}{3}\right)^n$. This series converges since $|r| = |1/3| < 1$.

We calculate the limit: $$ \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{\frac{1}{3^n+n^2}}{\frac{1}{3^n}} = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{3^n}{3^n+n^2} $$ Using L'Hôpital's Rule (differentiating with respect to $n$): $$ \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{(\ln 3)3^n}{(\ln 3)3^n + 2n} $$ Repeating L'Hôpital's Rule: $$ \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{(\ln 3)^2 3^n}{(\ln 3)^2 3^n + 2} $$ Repeating L'Hôpital's Rule once more: $$ \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{(\ln 3)^3 3^n}{(\ln 3)^3 3^n} = 1 $$ Since $0 < 1 < \infty$ and the geometric series converges, the series $\sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{3^n+n^2}$ also converges by the Limit Comparison Test.

2. Absolute Convergence

Definition: A series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} b_n$ is said to converge absolutely if the series of absolute values $\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} |b_n|$ converges.

Theorem: Absolute convergence implies convergence.
Example 2: Show that $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{n^2}$ converges.
We consider the absolute value of the terms: $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left| \frac{(-1)^n}{n^2} \right| = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} $$ This is a $p$-series with $p=2 > 1$. Therefore, the series converges absolutely.

Since $\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{n^2}$ converges absolutely, the series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{n^2}$ converges.

Example 3: Show that $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n^2+n+7}$ converges.
We consider the absolute value of the terms: $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left| \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n^2+n+7} \right| = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2+n+7} $$ For all $n > 0$, we have the inequality: $$ \frac{1}{n^2+n+7} \le \frac{1}{n^2} $$ since $n^2+n+7$ is larger than $n^2$.
The $p$-series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2}$ converges ($p=2 > 1$). By the direct comparison test, the series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n^2+n+7}$ of converges absolutely.

Therefore, the series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n^2+n+7}$ converges.
