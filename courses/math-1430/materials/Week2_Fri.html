Radius of Convergence

Calculus 2 Theory & Intervals Instructor Notes
Learning Objectives
  • Find the Center and Radius of Convergence of a power series.
  • Find the Interval of Convergence (checking endpoints).

1. The Process

For a power series defined as $f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n(x-a)^n$, there is a two-step process:

  1. Find the Center ($a$) and Radius ($R$) of convergence.
  2. Find the Interval of convergence (testing endpoints).
Finding R

Calculate one of the following limits based on the coefficients $a_n$:

$$ \lim_{n \to \infty} \left| \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} \right| = L \quad \text{OR} \quad \lim_{n \to \infty} \sqrt[n]{|a_n|} = L $$

The Three Cases:

  • I: If $L = \infty$, then $R = 0$. (Converges only at center).
  • II: If $L = 0$, then $R = \infty$. (Converges everywhere).
  • III: If $0 < L < \infty$, then $R = 1/L$.

2. Why does it work? (Geometric Intuition)

We justify these tests by comparing the "tail" of our power series to a Geometric Series.

Consider the ratio of entire consecutive terms (not just coefficients):

$$ \left| \frac{\text{Term}_{n+1}}{\text{Term}_n} \right| = \left| \frac{a_{n+1}(x-a)^{n+1}}{a_n(x-a)^n} \right| = \underbrace{\left| \frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n} \right|}_{\text{approaches } L} \cdot |x-a| $$

As $n \to \infty$, the ratio of coefficients settles down to the number $L$. Therefore, for very large $n$, the ratio of the entire series approximates:

$$ \text{Ratio} \approx L \cdot |x-a| $$

This means that eventually, the series behaves exactly like a Geometric Series with common ratio $L(x-a)$.

The Connection

A Geometric Series converges if the common ratio is less than one. Since our series resembles one, it also converges if:

$$ |L(x-a)| < 1 $$

Solving for $|x-a|$ gives the radius:

$$ |x-a| < \frac{1}{L} \implies R = \frac{1}{L} $$

3. Visualizing Convergence

From the inequality $|x-a| < 1/L$, we get an interval centered at $a$.

Diverges   Converges   Diverges
<---(xxxxx|xxxxx)--->
    a-R     a     a+R

Key Concept:

  • Inside the interval, the geometric ratio is "small" ($<1$). The series sums to a finite value.
  • Outside the interval, the geometric ratio is "large" ($>1$). The terms explode to infinity.

4. The Domain (Interval of Convergence)

Q: What is the domain of a power series?

A: An interval of the following six possible forms:

If R is finite ($0 < R < \infty$)
  • $(a-R, a+R)$
  • $[a-R, a+R)$
  • $(a-R, a+R]$
  • $[a-R, a+R]$
Special Cases

If $R = \infty$: $(-\infty, \infty)$

If $R = 0$: $[a, a]$ (Just the point)

Important: The Ratio and Root tests are inconclusive at the endpoints because the geometric ratio becomes exactly $r=1$. You must check them separately.

5. Testing Endpoints

Notation: When evaluating at an endpoint $x=b$, we plug $b$ into the series. Let $b_n = a_n(b-a)^n$. Thus, $f(b) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} b_n$.

  • If $x=b$ is in the domain, we say $\sum b_n$ converges.
  • If $x=b$ is NOT in the domain, we say $\sum b_n$ diverges.
Tools for Endpoint Testing

When checking endpoints, we get a numerical series. We check convergence using our previous tests:

  1. Divergence Test
  2. Alternating Series Test (Very common at endpoints)
  3. Comparison Tests / Limit Comparison
  4. Integral Tests / p-series

6. Review: Geometric Series Behavior

Since the Power Series tests rely on Geometric Series intuition, recall:

Series Example Reasoning Conclusion
$\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} 5^n$ Common ratio $|x| = 5 \ge 1$ Diverges
$\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \left(\frac{1}{10}\right)^n$ Common ratio $|x| = 0.1 < 1$ Converges
$\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} (-1)^n$ Common ratio $|x| = 1$ Diverges (Oscillates)
Final Remark Term-by-term integration and differentiation preserve the center and radius, but the convergence at endpoints can change.
