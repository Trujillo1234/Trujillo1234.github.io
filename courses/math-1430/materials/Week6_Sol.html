Calculus 2 • Interval of Convergence (Power Series)

1. Find the Interval of Convergence

(a) $$\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{(x-3)^n}{n}$$

center    $a=3$        $a_n=\dfrac{1}{n}$

Ratio Test

$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{\frac{1}{n+1}}{\frac{1}{n}}\right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{n}{n+1}\right|. $$
L'Hôpital's Rule:
$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n}{n+1} \ \xrightarrow{\text{L'H}} \ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{1} =1. $$

Radius is $R=1$.

Endpoints: $x=a-R$ and $x=a+R$

$$ x=3-1 \Rightarrow x=2, \qquad x=3+1 \Rightarrow x=4. $$

When $x=2$

$$ f(2)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{(2-3)^n}{n} = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^n}{n}. $$

Let $b_n=\dfrac{1}{n}$.

  1. For all $n>1$, $b_n=\dfrac{1}{n}>0$.
  2. For all $n>1$, $b_{n+1}=\dfrac{1}{n+1}\le \dfrac{1}{n}=b_n$.
  3. $\displaystyle \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n}=0$.

Since (a), (b), and (c) are all true, the alternating series test shows that $$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^n}{n} \text{ converges.}$$ Thus we should include $x=2$ in the interval of convergence.

When $x=4$

$$ f(4)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{(4-3)^n}{n} = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n}. $$

This is a $p$-series with $p=1$. Since $p\le 1$, the series diverges.

Thus, we should not include $x=4$ in the interval of convergence.

Interval of convergence: $$[2,4).$$

(b) $$\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{n!(x-2)^n}{n+1}$$

center    $a=2$        $a_n=\dfrac{n!}{n+1}$

Ratio Test

$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{\frac{(n+1)!}{n+2}}{\frac{n!}{n+1}}\right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{(n+1)!}{n+2}\cdot\frac{n+1}{n!}\right|. $$ $$ = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{(n+1)\,n!}{n+2}\cdot\frac{n+1}{n!}\right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{(n+1)^2}{n+2}\right|. $$
L'Hôpital's Rule:
$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{(n+1)^2}{n+2} \ \xrightarrow{\text{L'H}} \ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{2(n+1)}{1} =\infty. $$

The radius of convergence is $R=0$.

The interval of convergence: $$[2,2].$$

(c) $$\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{n^2(x-5)^n}{n!}$$

center    $a=5$        $a_n=\dfrac{n^2}{n!}$

Ratio Test

$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{\frac{(n+1)^2}{(n+1)!}}{\frac{n^2}{n!}}\right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{(n+1)^2}{(n+1)!}\cdot\frac{n!}{n^2}\right|. $$ $$ = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{(n+1)^2}{n^2}\cdot\frac{n!}{(n+1)!}\right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{(n+1)^2}{n^2}\cdot\frac{1}{n+1}\right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{(n+1)^2}{n^3+n^2}\right|. $$
L'Hôpital's Rule (first time):
$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{(n+1)^2}{n^3+n^2} \ \xrightarrow{\text{L'H}} \ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{2(n+1)}{3n^2+2n}. $$
L'Hôpital's Rule (second time):
$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{2(n+1)}{3n^2+2n} \ \xrightarrow{\text{L'H}} \ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{2}{6n+2} =0. $$

Radius of convergence: $R=\infty$.

The interval of convergence: $$(-\infty,\infty).$$

(d) $$\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{n(x+2)^n}{3^n}$$

center    $a=-2$        $a_n=\dfrac{n}{3^n}$

$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{\frac{n+1}{3^{n+1}}}{\frac{n}{3^n}}\right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{n+1}{3^{n+1}}\cdot\frac{3^n}{n}\right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{n+1}{3n}\right|. $$
L'Hôpital's Rule:
$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n+1}{3n} \ \xrightarrow{\text{L'H}} \ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{3} =\frac{1}{3}. $$

Radius of convergence: $R=3$.

Endpoints:

$$ x=a-R=-2-3=-5, \qquad x=a+R=-2+3=1. $$

When $x=-5$

$$ f(-5)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{n(-5+2)^n}{3^n} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{n(-3)^n}{3^n} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}n(-1)^n. $$

Note that since $\lim_{n\to\infty} n=\infty$, $\lim_{n\to\infty} (-1)^n n$ does not exist. By the divergence test, $\sum n(-1)^n$ diverges.

Thus, we should not include $x=-5$ in the interval of convergence.

When $x=1$

$$ f(1)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{n(1+2)^n}{3^n} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{n\cdot 3^n}{3^n} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}n. $$

Since $\lim_{n\to\infty} n=\infty\ne 0$, the divergence test implies that $\sum n$ diverges.

Thus, we should not include $x=1$ in the interval of convergence.

Interval of convergence: $$(-5,1).$$

(e) $$\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{4^n x^n}{\sqrt{n+1}}$$

center    $a=0$        $a_n=\dfrac{4^n}{\sqrt{n+1}}$

Ratio Test

$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{\frac{4^{n+1}}{\sqrt{n+2}}}{\frac{4^n}{\sqrt{n+1}}}\right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|4\cdot\frac{\sqrt{n+1}}{\sqrt{n+2}}\right| = 4\sqrt{\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n+1}{n+2}} =4\sqrt{1} =4. $$

The radius of convergence is $R=\dfrac{1}{4}$.

Endpoints:

$$ x=a-R=0-\frac14=-\frac14, \qquad x=a+R=0+\frac14=\frac14. $$

When $x=\dfrac14$

$$ f\!\left(\frac14\right) =\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{4^n\left(\frac14\right)^n}{\sqrt{n+1}} =\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt{n+1}}. $$

Compare to $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}$, a $p$-series with $p=\frac12$. Since $p\le 1$, the $p$-series diverges.

Limit comparison computation:

$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\frac{1}{\sqrt{n+1}}}{\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}} = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\sqrt{n}}{\sqrt{n+1}} = \sqrt{\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n}{n+1}}. $$
L'Hôpital's Rule:
$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n}{n+1} \ \xrightarrow{\text{L'H}} \ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{1} =1 \quad\Rightarrow\quad \sqrt{1}=1. $$

Since $0<1<\infty$, the limit comparison test implies that $$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt{n+1}} \text{ must also diverge.}$$ Thus, we should not include $x=\dfrac14$ in the interval of convergence.

When $x=-\dfrac14$

$$ f\!\left(-\frac14\right) =\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{4^n\left(-\frac14\right)^n}{\sqrt{n+1}} =\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^n}{\sqrt{n+1}}. $$

Alt. Series Test: let $b_n=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{n+1}}$.

  1. For all $n>1$, $\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{n+1}}>0$.
  2. For all $n>1$, $b_{n+1}=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{n+2}}<\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{n+1}}=b_n$.
  3. $\displaystyle \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt{n+1}}=0$.

Since (a), (b), and (c) are all true, the alternating series test implies that $$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^n}{\sqrt{n+1}} \text{ converges.}$$ Thus, we should include $x=-\dfrac14$ in the interval of convergence.

Interval of convergence: $$\left[-\frac14,\frac14\right).$$

(f) $$\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}2^n n^2 x^n$$

center    $a=0$        $a_n=2^n n^2$

Ratio Test

$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{2^{n+1}(n+1)^2}{2^n n^2}\right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|2\cdot\frac{(n+1)^2}{n^2}\right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{2(n+1)^2}{n^2}\right|. $$
L'Hôpital's Rule (as written in the key):
$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{2(n+1)^2}{n^2} = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{4(n+1)}{2n} \ \xrightarrow{\text{L'H}} \ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{4}{2} =2. $$

The radius of convergence is $R=\dfrac12$.

Endpoints:

$$ x=a-R=0-\frac12=-\frac12, \qquad x=a+R=0+\frac12=\frac12. $$

When $x=\dfrac12$

$$ f\!\left(\frac12\right)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}2^n n^2\left(\frac12\right)^n =\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}n^2. $$

Since $\lim_{n\to\infty}n^2=\infty\ne 0$, the divergence test implies that $\sum n^2$ diverges. So we should not include this endpoint in the interval of convergence.

When $x=-\dfrac12$

$$ f\!\left(-\frac12\right)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}2^n n^2\left(-\frac12\right)^n =\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}(-1)^n n^2. $$

Since $\lim_{n\to\infty}n^2=\infty$, $\lim_{n\to\infty}(-1)^n n^2$ does not exist. By the divergence test, $\sum (-1)^n n^2$ diverges.

Thus, we should not include $x=-\dfrac12$ in the interval of convergence.

Interval of convergence: $$\left(-\frac12,\frac12\right).$$

(g) $$\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}n^n x^n$$

center    $a=0$        $a_n=n^n$

Root test:

$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\sqrt[n]{|a_n|} = \lim_{n\to\infty}\sqrt[n]{n^n} = \lim_{n\to\infty}n =\infty. $$

Radius of convergence: $R=0$.

Interval of convergence: $$[0,0].$$

(h) $$\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(x-2)^n}{n^2+1}$$

center    $a=2$        $a_n=\dfrac{1}{n^2+1}$

Ratio Test

$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{\frac{1}{(n+1)^2+1}}{\frac{1}{n^2+1}}\right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{n^2+1}{(n+1)^2+1}\right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n^2+1}{n^2+2n+2}. $$
L'Hôpital's Rule (first time):
$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n^2+1}{n^2+2n+2} \ \xrightarrow{\text{L'H}} \ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{2n}{2n+2}. $$
L'Hôpital's Rule (second time):
$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{2n}{2n+2} \ \xrightarrow{\text{L'H}} \ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{2}{2} =1. $$

Radius of convergence: $R=1$.

Endpoints:

$$ x=a-R=2-1=1, \qquad x=a+R=2+1=3. $$

When $x=1$

$$ f(1)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(1-2)^n}{n^2+1} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^n}{n^2+1}. $$

Alt. series test: let $b_n=\dfrac{1}{n^2+1}$.

  1. For all $n>1$, $b_n=\dfrac{1}{n^2+1}>0$.
  2. For all $n>1$, $$b_{n+1}=\frac{1}{(n+1)^2+1}<\frac{1}{n^2+1}=b_n.$$
  3. $$\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n^2+1}=0.$$

Since (a), (b), and (c) are all true, the alternating series test shows that $$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^n}{n^2+1}\ \text{converges.}$$ Thus, we should include $x=1$ in the interval of convergence.

When $x=3$

$$ f(3)=\sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{(3-2)^n}{n^2+1} = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^2+1}. $$

Compare to the series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^2}$, a $p$-series with $p=2$. Since $p>1$, it converges.

Limit comparison computation:

$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\frac{1}{n^2+1}}{\frac{1}{n^2}} = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n^2}{n^2+1}. $$
L'Hôpital's Rule:
$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n^2}{n^2+1} \ \xrightarrow{\text{L'H}} \ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{2n}{2n} =1. $$

By the limit comparison test, $\displaystyle \sum \frac{1}{n^2+1}$ must also converge. So we should include $x=3$ in the interval of convergence.

Interval of convergence: $$[1,3].$$

(i) $$\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{n}{2^n(n^2+1)}\,x^n$$

center    $a=0$        $a_n=\dfrac{n}{2^n(n^2+1)}$

Ratio Test

$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left| \frac{\frac{n+1}{2^{n+1}((n+1)^2+1)}}{\frac{n}{2^n(n^2+1)}} \right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left| \frac{n+1}{2^{n+1}((n+1)^2+1)}\cdot \frac{2^n(n^2+1)}{n} \right|. $$ $$ = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left| \frac{(n+1)(n^2+1)}{2((n+1)^2+1)n} \right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left| \frac{(n+1)(n^2+1)}{2(n^2+2n+2)n} \right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left| \frac{n^3+n^2+n+1}{2(n^3+2n^2+2n)} \right|. $$
L'Hôpital's Rule (first time):
$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n^3+n^2+n+1}{2(n^3+2n^2+2n)} \ \xrightarrow{\text{L'H}} \ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{3n^2+2n+1}{2(3n^2+4n+2)}. $$
L'Hôpital's Rule (second time):
$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{3n^2+2n+1}{2(3n^2+4n+2)} \ \xrightarrow{\text{L'H}} \ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{6n+2}{2(6n+4)}. $$
L'Hôpital's Rule (third time):
$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{6n+2}{2(6n+4)} \ \xrightarrow{\text{L'H}} \ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{6}{12} =\frac{1}{2}. $$

Radius of convergence: $R=2$.

Endpoints:

$$ x=a-R=0-2=-2, \qquad x=a+R=0+2=2. $$

When $x=2$

$$ f(2)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{n}{2^n(n^2+1)}\,2^n = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{n}{n^2+1}. $$

Compare to the $p$-series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n}$. This is a diverging $p$-series since $p=1$ and $p\le 1$.

Limit comparison computation:

$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{\frac{n}{n^2+1}}{\frac{1}{n}} = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n}{n^2+1}\cdot n = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n^2}{n^2+1}. $$
L'Hôpital's Rule:
$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n^2}{n^2+1} \ \xrightarrow{\text{L'H}} \ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{2n}{2n} =1. $$

Since $0<1<\infty$, the limit comparison test shows that $$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{n}{n^2+1} \text{ must also diverge.}$$ Thus, we should not include $x=2$ in the interval of convergence.

When $x=-2$

$$ f(-2)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{n}{2^n(n^2+1)}\,(-2)^n = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{n}{2^n(n^2+1)}\,2^n(-1)^n = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}(-1)^n\frac{n}{n^2+1}. $$

Alt. series test: let $b_n=\dfrac{n}{n^2+1}$.

  1. For all $n>1$, $b_n=\dfrac{n}{n^2+1}>0$.
  2. Show $b_n$ is decreasing for $n>1$ using the derivative test: $$ \left(\frac{n}{n^2+1}\right)' = \frac{(n^2+1)\cdot 1 - n(2n)}{(n^2+1)^2} = \frac{-n^2+1}{(n^2+1)^2} <0 \quad \text{when } n>1. $$ Thus, $b_{n+1}<b_n$ for all $n>1$.
  3. $$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n}{n^2+1} = \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n+\frac{1}{n}}. $$
    L'Hôpital's Rule (as written in the key):
    $$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n}{n^2+1} \ \xrightarrow{\text{L'H}} \ \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{2n} =0. $$

Since (a), (b), and (c) are all true, the alternating series test implies convergence at $x=-2$.

Thus, we should include $x=-2$ in the interval of convergence.

Interval of convergence: $$[-2,2).$$

(j) $$\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{(x-4)^n}{n^3}$$

center    $a=4$        $a_n=\dfrac{1}{n^3}$

Ratio Test

$$ \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{a_{n+1}}{a_n}\right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left|\frac{1}{(n+1)^3}\cdot\frac{n^3}{1}\right| = \lim_{n\to\infty}\left(\frac{n}{n+1}\right)^3. $$
L'Hôpital's Rule:
$$ =\left(\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n}{n+1}\right)^3 \ \xrightarrow{\text{L'H}} \ \left(\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{1}\right)^3 =1^3 = 1. $$

So the interval of convergence is $R=1$.

Endpoints:

$$ x=a-R=4-1=3, \qquad x=a+R=4+1=5. $$

When $x=5$

$$ f(5)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{(5-4)^n}{n^3} = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^3}. $$

This is a $p$-series with $p=3$. Since $p>1$, the series converges by the $p$-series test. So we should include $x=5$ in the interval of convergence.

When $x=3$

$$ f(3)=\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{(3-4)^n}{n^3} = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^n}{n^3}. $$

Note that $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^\infty \left| \frac{(-1)^n}{n^3} \right| = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^3}$ is a convergent $p$-series. Thus, $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{(-1)^n}{n^3}$ absolutely converges. So, we should include $x=3$ in the interval of convergence.

Interval of convergence: $$[3,5].$$

3. Determine whether the series is absolutely convergent, conditionally convergent, or divergent

(a)
$$ \displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left| \frac{(-1)^n}{n} \right| = \displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n} \quad \text{is a } p\text{-series with } p=1. $$ Since $p \le 1$, it diverges. Thus $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{n}$ is not absolutely convergent.

Alternating Series Test: Let $b_n = \frac{1}{n}$.
  1. For all $n>1$, $b_n = \frac{1}{n} > 0$.
  2. For all $n>1$, $b_{n+1} = \frac{1}{n+1} \le \frac{1}{n} = b_n$.
  3. $\displaystyle \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{n} = 0$.
By the alternating series test, $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{n}$ converges. Since the series converges but does not absolutely converge, $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{n}$ is conditionally convergent.
(b)
$$ \displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left| \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n^2} \right| = \displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} \quad \text{is a } p\text{-series with } p=2. $$ Since $p > 1$, the $p$-series converges. Thus, $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n^2}$ absolutely converges.
(c)
$$ \displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left| \frac{(-1)^n}{\sqrt{n}} \right| = \displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} \quad \text{is a } p\text{-series with } p=\frac{1}{2}. $$ Since $p \le 1$, the $p$-series diverges. Thus, $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{\sqrt{n}}$ does not absolutely converge.

Now consider the alternating series test with $b_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}$.
  1. For all $n>1$, $b_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} > 0$.
  2. For all $n>1$, $b_{n+1} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n+1}} \le \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} = b_n$.
  3. $\displaystyle \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}} = 0$.
Since (a), (b), and (c) are all true, the alternating series test shows $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{\sqrt{n}}$ converges. Since the series converges but does not absolutely converge, $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{\sqrt{n}}$ converges conditionally.
(d)
$$ \displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left| \frac{(-1)^n}{n^2+1} \right| = \displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2+1}. $$ Compare to the $p$-series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2}$. Since $p=2>1$, this $p$-series converges. $$ \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{\frac{1}{n^2+1}}{\frac{1}{n^2}} = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{n^2}{n^2+1} = 1. $$ Since $0<1<\infty$, the limit comparison test shows $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2+1}$ must also converge. Thus, $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{n^2+1}$ converges absolutely.
(e)
$$ \displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left| \frac{1}{n^{1.5}} \right| = \displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^{1.5}} \quad \text{is a } p\text{-series with } p=1.5. $$ Since $1.5 > 1$, the $p$-series converges. Thus, $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^{1.5}}$ converges absolutely.
(f)
$$ \displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left| \frac{(-1)^n}{2^n+1} \right| = \displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{2^n+1}. $$ For all $n>1$, $$0 < \frac{1}{2^n+1} \le \frac{1}{2^n} = \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^n.$$ The series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^n$ is a geometric series with $x=\frac{1}{2}$. Since $|\frac{1}{2}|<1$ the geometric series converges. By the direct comparison test, $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{2^n+1}$ must also converge. Therefore $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{2^n+1}$ converges absolutely.
(g)
$$ \displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left| \frac{(-1)^n}{\sqrt{n}+2} \right| = \displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}+2}. $$ Compare to the $p$-series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}$. Since $p=\frac{1}{2} \le 1$, this $p$-series diverges. $$ \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}}{\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}+2}} = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{\sqrt{n}+2}{\sqrt{n}} = 1. $$ Since $0 < 1 < \infty$, the limit comparison test shows the series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}+2}$ must also diverge. Thus, $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{\sqrt{n}+2}$ is not absolutely convergent.

Now consider the alternating series test with $b_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}+2}$.
  1. For all $n>1$, $b_n = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}+2} > 0$.
  2. For all $n>1$, $b_{n+1} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n+1}+2} \le \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}+2} = b_n$.
  3. $\displaystyle \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}+2} = 0$.
Since (a), (b), and (c) are all true, $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{\sqrt{n}+2}$ converges. Since this series converges but does not absolutely converge, it converges conditionally.
(h)
$$ \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{n^3}{n^3+1} \ \xrightarrow{\text{L'H}} \ \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{3n^2}{3n^2} = 1. $$ Since $1 \ne 0$, the divergence test shows that $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{n^3}{n^3+1}$ diverges.

4. Power Series Expansions

First recall the following four power series centered at $x=0$.

$$e^x = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{x^n}{n!}$$ $$\sin(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!}$$ $$\cos(x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n}}{(2n)!}$$ $$\frac{1}{1-x} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty x^n \quad (\text{when } |x|<1).$$
(a)
$$ \cos(x^2) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n (x^2)^{2n}}{(2n)!} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{4n}}{(2n)!} $$
(b)
$$ \cos(x^3) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n (x^3)^{2n}}{(2n)!} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{6n}}{(2n)!} $$
(c)
$$ e^{x^2} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(x^2)^n}{n!} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{x^{2n}}{n!} $$
(d)
$$ e^{2x} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(2x)^n}{n!} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{2^n x^n}{n!} $$
(e)
$$ \sin(5x) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n (5x)^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!} $$
(f)
$$ \sin(x^2) = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n (x^2)^{2n+1}}{(2n+1)!} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{4n+2}}{(2n+1)!} $$
(g)
$$ \frac{1}{1+2x} = \frac{1}{1-(-2x)} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty (-2x)^n = \sum_{n=0}^\infty (-2)^n x^n $$
(h)
$$ \frac{1}{1-5x} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty (5x)^n = \sum_{n=0}^\infty 5^n x^n $$
(i)
$$ \frac{1}{1+x^2} = \frac{1}{1-(-x^2)} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty (-x^2)^n = \sum_{n=0}^\infty (-1)^n x^{2n} $$
(j)
$$ \frac{1}{1-x^3} = \sum_{n=0}^\infty (x^3)^n = \sum_{n=0}^\infty x^{3n} $$
(k)
$$ \frac{8}{1-x^2} = 8 \cdot \frac{1}{1-x^2} = 8 \sum_{n=0}^\infty (x^2)^n = \sum_{n=0}^\infty 8 \cdot x^{2n} $$

5. Integrals and Derivatives of Power Series

(a)
$$ \int \cos(x^2) dx = C + \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{4n+1}}{(2n)! (4n+1)} $$ $$ (\cos(x^2))' = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n 4n x^{4n-1}}{(2n)!} $$
(b)
$$ \int \cos(x^3) dx = C + \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{6n+1}}{(2n)! (6n+1)} $$ $$ (\cos(x^3))' = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n \cdot 6n \cdot x^{6n-1}}{(2n)!} $$
(c)
$$ \int e^{x^2} dx = C + \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{x^{2n+1}}{n! (2n+1)} $$ $$ (e^{x^2})' = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{2n \cdot x^{2n-1}}{n!} $$
(d)
$$ \int e^{2x} dx = C + \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{2^n x^{n+1}}{n! (n+1)} $$ $$ (e^{2x})' = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{2^n \cdot n \cdot x^{n-1}}{n!} $$
(e)
$$ \int \sin(5x) dx = C + \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n 5^{2n+1} x^{2n+2}}{(2n+1)! (2n+2)} $$ $$ (\sin(5x))' = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n \cdot 5^{2n+1} (2n+1) x^{2n}}{(2n+1)!} $$
(f)
$$ \int \sin(x^2) dx = C + \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{4n+3}}{(2n+1)! (4n+3)} $$ $$ (\sin(x^2))' = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n (4n+2) x^{4n+1}}{(2n+1)!} $$
(g)
$$ \int \frac{1}{1+2x} dx = C + \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-2)^n x^{n+1}}{n+1} $$ $$ \left(\frac{1}{1+2x}\right)' = \sum_{n=0}^\infty (-2)^n \cdot n \cdot x^{n-1} $$
(h)
$$ \int \frac{1}{1-5x} dx = C + \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{5^n x^{n+1}}{n+1} $$ $$ \left(\frac{1}{1-5x}\right)' = \sum_{n=0}^\infty 5^n \cdot n \cdot x^{n-1} $$
(i)
$$ \int \frac{1}{1+x^2} dx = C + \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{(-1)^n x^{2n+1}}{2n+1} $$ $$ \left(\frac{1}{1+x^2}\right)' = \sum_{n=0}^\infty (-1)^n \cdot 2n \cdot x^{2n-1} $$
(j)
$$ \int \frac{1}{1-x^3} dx = C + \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{x^{3n+1}}{3n+1} $$ $$ \left(\frac{1}{1-x^3}\right)' = \sum_{n=0}^\infty 3n \cdot x^{3n-1} $$
(k)
$$ \int \frac{8}{1-x^2} dx = C + \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{8 x^{2n+1}}{2n+1} $$ $$ \left(\frac{8}{1-x^2}\right)' = \sum_{n=0}^\infty 16 n x^{2n-1} $$
