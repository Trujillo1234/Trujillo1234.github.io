Learning Objectives
  • Use the direct comparison test to show that a series converges or diverges
  • Use the limit comparison test to show that a series converges or diverges

1. Stronger Benchmarks of Comparisons

Constant multiple of a $p$-series $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{C}{n^p} $$
  • $p > 1$: Converges
  • $p \le 1$: Diverges
Where $C$ is some fixed constant value.
Constant multiple of a geometric series $$ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} C x^n $$
  • $|x| < 1$: Converges
  • $|x| \ge 1$: Diverges
Where $C$ is some fixed constant value.

2. Comparison Test Examples

Example 1: Determine if $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{n+5}{n^2+5n+1}$ converges or diverges.
We use the Limit Comparison Test. For large values of $n$, the dominant terms in the numerator and denominator determine the behavior of the series. Since $$ \frac{n+5}{n^2+5n+1} \sim \frac{n}{n^2} = \frac{1}{n}, $$ we compare the series to the harmonic series $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n}. $$ The harmonic series diverges because it is a $p$-series with $p=1 \le 1$. Both series have positive terms for $n \ge 1$.

We define the limit $L$: $$ L = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{\frac{n+5}{n^2+5n+1}}{\frac{1}{n}} = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{n+5}{n^2+5n+1}\cdot n $$ $$ L = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{n^2+5n}{n^2+5n+1}. $$ Using L'Hôpital's Rule, $$ L = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{2n+5}{2n+5} = 1. $$ Thus $0< L <\infty$. By the Limit Comparison Test, since the harmonic series diverges, the series $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{n+5}{n^2+5n+1} $$ also diverges.

Example 2: Determine if $\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{9}{7^n+5n+1}$ converges or diverges.
We use the Direct Comparison Test. The exponential term $7^n$ dominates the denominator for large $n$, so we compare the series to the geometric series $$ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} 9\left(\frac{1}{7}\right)^n. $$ The geometric series converges because $|r|=\frac{1}{7}<1$.

For all $n\ge 0$, $$ 0 < \frac{9}{7^n+5n+1} \le \frac{9}{7^n} $$ since $7^n+5n+1 \ge 7^n$.

Thus by the Direct Comparison Test, since the geometric series $$ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} 9\left(\frac{1}{7}\right)^n $$ converges, the series $$ \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{9}{7^n+5n+1} $$ also converges.

Example 3: Determine if $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{\sqrt{n}}{n+5}$ converges or diverges.
We use the Limit Comparison Test. For large $n$, the dominant terms determine the behavior: $$ \frac{\sqrt{n}}{n+5} \sim \frac{\sqrt{n}}{n} = \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}. $$ We therefore compare to the $p$-series $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}. $$ This is a $p$-series with $p=\tfrac12 \le 1$, so it diverges. Both series have positive terms for $n \ge 1$.

We define the limit $L$: $$ L=\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{\frac{\sqrt{n}}{n+5}}{\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}} = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{\sqrt{n}}{n+5}\cdot \sqrt{n} $$ $$ L=\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{n}{n+5}. $$ Using L'Hôpital's Rule, $$ L=\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{1}=1. $$ Thus $0< L <\infty$. By the Limit Comparison Test, since the comparison $p$-series diverges, the series $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{\sqrt{n}}{n+5} $$ also diverges.
