The Divergence Test

Calculus 2 Monday, Feb 2 Instructor Notes
Learning Objectives
  • Apply the divergence test to show that a series diverges.
  • Recognize when the divergence test is inconclusive.
How to apply a convergence/divergence test (you are making an argument)
  • State the test you are using (by name) and what it will let you conclude.
  • Verify the hypotheses of that test using clear sentences (or bullets): check each condition the test requires.
  • Conclude about a specific series by writing the series explicitly (avoid vague pronouns like “this series”).

1. Review: Domain of Power Series

Q: What is the domain of a power series?
A: An interval with center $x=a$ and radius $R$.

Q: Do we include the endpoints $x=a+R$ or $x=a-R$ in the domain?
A: Sometimes yes and sometimes no; we must test the endpoints separately.

2. Definition of Infinite Series

Definition

An infinite series is an expression of the form:

$$ \displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} b_n = b_0 + b_1 + b_2 + \dots $$

We say that the series converges if the following limit exists:

$$ \displaystyle \lim_{n \to \infty} (b_0 + b_1 + b_2 + \dots + b_n) $$

If the limit does not exist, we say the series diverges.

3. The Divergence Test

Theorem: The Divergence Test

If $\displaystyle \lim_{n \to \infty} b_n \neq 0$, then $\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} b_n$ diverges.

If $\displaystyle \lim_{n \to \infty} b_n = 0$, then the test is inconclusive.

Warning: “Inconclusive” means the Divergence Test cannot decide; you must use a different test.
Proof Sketch

Suppose that $\sum b_n$ converges to a sum $L$. Let $S_n = b_0 + \dots + b_n$.

Note that $b_n = S_n - S_{n-1}$. Taking the limit:

$$ \displaystyle \lim_{n \to \infty} b_n = \lim_{n \to \infty} (S_n - S_{n-1}) = L - L = 0 $$

Thus, if the series converges, the term limit must be 0. Logically, this implies: if the term limit is not 0, then the series cannot converge.

Worked Examples

Example A

Question: Determine whether $\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{3n+1}{4n-2}$ converges or diverges.

Test Used: Divergence Test.

Hypotheses to check: We must compute $\displaystyle \lim_{n\to\infty} b_n$ where $b_n=\frac{3n+1}{4n-2}$.

Limit computation: Substituting $n\to\infty$ gives an indeterminate form $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$, so L'Hôpital's Rule applies.

$$ \displaystyle \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{3n+1}{4n-2} \overset{L'H}{=} \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{\frac{d}{dn}(3n+1)}{\frac{d}{dn}(4n-2)} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{3}{4} = \frac{3}{4}. $$

Conclusion: Since $\displaystyle \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{3n+1}{4n-2}=\frac{3}{4}\neq 0$, the Divergence Test implies that $\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{3n+1}{4n-2}$ diverges.

Example B

Question: Determine whether $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{n^2}{3n^2-1}$ converges or diverges.

Test Used: Divergence Test.

Hypotheses to check: We must compute $\displaystyle \lim_{n\to\infty} b_n$ where $b_n=\frac{n^2}{3n^2-1}$.

Limit computation: Substituting $n\to\infty$ gives an indeterminate form $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$, so L'Hôpital's Rule applies.

$$ \displaystyle \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{n^2}{3n^2-1} \overset{L'H}{=} \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{2n}{6n}. $$

At this stage the expression $\frac{2n}{6n}$ is still of the form $\frac{\infty}{\infty}$, but we can simplify by canceling the common factor $n$ (valid for $n\neq 0$):

$$ \displaystyle \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{2n}{6n} = \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{2}{6} = \frac{1}{3}. $$

Conclusion: Since $\displaystyle \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{n^2}{3n^2-1}=\frac{1}{3}\neq 0$, the Divergence Test implies that $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{n^2}{3n^2-1}$ diverges.

Example C

Question: Determine whether $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^3}$ converges or diverges.

Test Used: Divergence Test (attempt).

Hypotheses to check: We compute $\displaystyle \lim_{n\to\infty} b_n$ where $b_n=\frac{1}{n^3}$.

Limit computation: As $n\to\infty$, we have $n^3\to\infty$, so $\frac{1}{n^3}$ has the form $\frac{1}{\infty}$ (not an indeterminate form). Since the denominator grows without bound while the numerator stays fixed, the fraction must go to 0:

$$ \displaystyle \lim_{n \to \infty} \frac{1}{n^3} = 0. $$

Conclusion: Because $\displaystyle \lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n^3}=0$, the Divergence Test is inconclusive for $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^3}$. The Divergence Test cannot decide whether $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^3}$ converges or diverges.

Example D

Question: Determine whether $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \cos\!\left(\frac{1}{n^2}\right)$ converges or diverges.

Test Used: Divergence Test.

Hypotheses to check: We compute $\displaystyle \lim_{n\to\infty} b_n$ where $b_n=\cos\!\left(\frac{1}{n^2}\right)$.

Limit computation: First note that $\frac{1}{n^2}\to 0$ as $n\to\infty$ (again this is of the form $\frac{1}{\infty}$, so the limit is 0).

Next use continuity: if $f$ is continuous, then $\lim f(x_n)=f(\lim x_n)$ (when the inside limit exists). Since $\cos(x)$ is continuous everywhere,

$$ \displaystyle \lim_{n \to \infty} \cos\!\left(\frac{1}{n^2}\right) = \cos\!\left(\lim_{n\to\infty}\frac{1}{n^2}\right) = \cos(0) = 1. $$

Conclusion: Since $\displaystyle \lim_{n\to\infty}\cos\!\left(\frac{1}{n^2}\right)=1\neq 0$, the Divergence Test implies that $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \cos\!\left(\frac{1}{n^2}\right)$ diverges.
