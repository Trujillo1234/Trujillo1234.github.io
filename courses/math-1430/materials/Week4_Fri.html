P-Series Test

Calculus 2 Thursday, Feb 5 Instructor Notes
Learning Objectives
  • Apply the p-series test, or state that it cannot be applied to the series.
Motivation

We want fast recognition tests for common benchmark series.

Many series can be identified quickly by matching a familiar pattern. One of the most important families is $$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^p}.$$ Once the series is in this exact form, the question “converges or diverges?” is decided immediately by the value of $p$.

How to apply a test (you are making an argument)
  • State the test you are using.
  • Verify the test applies (here: confirm the series is actually a p-series).
  • Conclude clearly by citing the relevant condition on $p$.
Definition: The Harmonic Series $$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n} = 1+\frac12+\frac13+\frac14+\frac15+\cdots$$

This is the p-series with $p=1$.

Why the Harmonic Series Diverges (Visual Idea)

Let $f(x)=\dfrac{1}{x}$. Since $f$ is positive and decreasing on $[1,\infty)$, the left-endpoint rectangles of width $1$ lie above the curve. This produces the key inequality:

$$\int_{1}^{n+1}\frac{1}{x}\,dx \le 1+\frac12+\frac13+\cdots+\frac{1}{n}.$$
The rectangles represent $1+\frac12+\cdots+\frac1n$, and the curve area represents $\int_{1}^{n+1}\frac1x\,dx$. The rectangles are larger, so the series partial sums are larger than the integral.

Compute the integral: $$\int_{1}^{n+1}\frac{1}{x}\,dx=\ln|x|\Big|_{1}^{n+1}=\ln(n+1).$$ Taking limits, $$\lim_{n\to\infty}\ln(n+1)=\infty.$$ Since $\ln(n+1)\le 1+\frac12+\cdots+\frac1n$ and the left side grows without bound, the harmonic series diverges.

The harmonic series diverges very slowly!!

A useful estimate is $$1+\frac12+\cdots+\frac1n \;\approx\; 1+\ln(n).$$ For example, if you add one term per second since the beginning of the universe (about $4\times 10^{17}$ seconds), then $$1+\frac12+\cdots+\frac{1}{4\times 10^{17}} \;\approx\; 1+\ln(4\times 10^{17}) \;\approx\; 41.5.$$

Even with absurd speed—say a top supercomputer effectively summing on the order of $10^{18}$ terms per second for the entire age of the universe— you would reach only about $$1+\ln(4\times 10^{35})\approx 83,$$ which is still “small” compared to what divergence might suggest.

Theorem: P-Series Test

A p-series is a series of the form

$$\sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^p}\quad\text{where $p$ is a real number.}$$

The p-series

  • converges when $p>1$
  • diverges when $p\le 1$
Proof Sketch (Integral Comparison Idea)

Let $f(x)=\dfrac{1}{x^p}=x^{-p}$, which is positive and decreasing for $x\ge 1$ when $p>0$. The same rectangle-vs-curve idea compares $$\sum_{k=1}^{n}\frac{1}{k^p}$$ with $$\int_{1}^{n}x^{-p}\,dx.$$

When $p\ne 1$: $$\int_{1}^{n}x^{-p}\,dx=\frac{x^{-p+1}}{-p+1}\Big|_{1}^{n} =\frac{n^{-p+1}-1}{-p+1}.$$

  • If $p>1$, then $-p+1<0$ so $n^{-p+1}\to 0$ and the integral approaches a finite value. This forces the p-series to converge.
  • If $p<1$, then $-p+1>0$ so $n^{-p+1}\to\infty$ and the integral diverges. This forces the p-series to diverge.
  • If $p=1$, the antiderivative is $\ln|x|$, which is exactly the harmonic-series case.

Worked Examples

Example 1

Question: Determine whether $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^2}$ converges or diverges.

Test Used: P-Series Test.

Verify the form: This is $\sum \frac{1}{n^p}$ with $p=2$.

Conclusion: Since $2>1$, the series converges.

Example 2

Question: Determine whether $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^{3/2}}$ converges or diverges.

Test Used: P-Series Test.

Verify the form: This is $\sum \frac{1}{n^p}$ with $p=\dfrac{3}{2}$.

Conclusion: Since $\dfrac{3}{2}>1$, the series converges.

Example 3

Question: Determine whether $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}$ converges or diverges.

Test Used: P-Series Test.

Rewrite: $\displaystyle \frac{1}{\sqrt{n}}=\frac{1}{n^{1/2}}$, so $p=\dfrac12$.

Conclusion: Since $\dfrac12\le 1$, the series diverges.

Example 4

Question: Determine whether $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}n^{-3}$ converges or diverges.

Test Used: P-Series Test.

Verify the form: This is $\sum \frac{1}{n^p}$ with $p=3$.

Conclusion: Since $3>1$, the series converges.

Example 5 (test cannot be applied)

Question: Can we use the p-series test on $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty}\frac{1}{n^2+1}$?

Check the form: A p-series must be exactly $\displaystyle \sum \frac{1}{n^p}$.

Conclusion: This is not in p-series form, so the p-series test cannot be applied.
