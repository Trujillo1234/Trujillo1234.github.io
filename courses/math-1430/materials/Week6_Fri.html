1. Absolute and Conditional Convergence

Definition (Absolute Convergence).

A series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} b_n$ is said to converge absolutely if $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} |b_n| $$ converges.

Important Fact.

If a series converges absolutely, then the original series also converges.

Definition (Conditional Convergence).

A series that converges but does not converge absolutely is said to converge conditionally.

Absolute convergence is a strong form of convergence. When the absolute values converge, the signs of the terms no longer matter — the total size of the terms is small enough to force convergence. Conditional convergence occurs when cancellation between positive and negative terms is essential for convergence.

This distinction becomes especially important for power series. Inside the interval of convergence, convergence is typically absolute. Conditional convergence can only occur at the endpoints, where the Ratio or Root Test becomes inconclusive.

2. Example: Alternating Harmonic Series

The alternating harmonic series is the fundamental example showing that convergence can depend on cancellation. The magnitudes of the terms alone are not small enough to produce convergence, but alternating signs allow the partial sums to settle toward a finite value.

Determine if the series $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{n} $$ converges absolutely, converges conditionally, or diverges.
First consider absolute convergence: $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left|\frac{(-1)^n}{n}\right| = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n}. $$ This is a $p$-series with $p=1$, which diverges. Therefore the series is not absolutely convergent.

Now apply the Alternating Series Test. Let $b_n=\frac{1}{n}$.
  1. $b_n>0$ for all $n\ge1$.
  2. $b_{n+1}=\frac{1}{n+1}\le \frac{1}{n}=b_n$.
  3. $\displaystyle \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{n}=0$.
Since all conditions are satisfied, the series converges by the Alternating Series Test.

Thus, $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^n}{n} $$ converges conditionally.

3. Example: Absolute Convergence by Comparison

When the absolute values of the terms can be compared to a known convergent series, we immediately obtain absolute convergence. In this case, the alternating sign plays no essential role.

Determine if the series $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n^2+n+2} $$ converges absolutely, converges conditionally, or diverges.
Consider absolute convergence: $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left|\frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n^2+n+2}\right| = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2+n+2}. $$ For $n>1$, $$ 0 < \frac{1}{n^2+n+2} \le \frac{1}{n^2}. $$ The series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2}$ converges.

By the Direct Comparison Test, $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2+n+2} $$ converges.

Therefore, $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{(-1)^{n+1}}{n^2+n+2} $$ converges absolutely.

4. Example: Absolute Convergence with a Bounded Function

Many series involve oscillating functions such as sine or cosine. Since these functions remain bounded between $-1$ and $1$, comparison with a $p$-series is often sufficient to establish absolute convergence.

Determine if the series $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{\cos(n)}{n^3} $$ converges or diverges.
Consider absolute convergence: $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \left|\frac{\cos(n)}{n^3}\right| = \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{|\cos(n)|}{n^3}. $$ Since $|\cos(n)| \le 1$, $$ 0 < \frac{|\cos(n)|}{n^3} \le \frac{1}{n^3}. $$ The series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^3}$ converges.

By the Direct Comparison Test, $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{|\cos(n)|}{n^3} $$ converges.

Therefore, $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{\cos(n)}{n^3} $$ converges absolutely.
