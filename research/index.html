Research Program

My research lies at the intersection of infinite combinatorics, topology, and logic. I primarily focus on Ramsey theory, topological Ramsey spaces, ultrafilters, Tukey theory, and nonstandard models of arithmetic. The central question driving my work is how complex, infinite objects retain usable structure once the right notions of reduction, classification, or homogeneity are applied.

I am drawn to problems where a theorem is not only a result but a way of seeing. A Ramsey theorem says that disorder contains organized substructure. A topological Ramsey space gives a setting where that phenomenon can be rebuilt beyond the classical Ellentuck space. Ultrafilters and Tukey reductions then ask how these structures compare, how much information they carry, and which maps between them preserve the essential mathematics.

Beyond my core research, I design projects that serve as an entry point for students. These range from abstract topics in set theory and logic to highly concrete problems involving voting, graph theory, numerical integration, dishware, or bridges. In each case, my goal is for students to experience mathematics as an active, authored process: crafting precise definitions, testing examples rigorously, and writing clear, inspectable arguments.

Graduate Work

My dissertation is the most direct source for my current research program: topological Ramsey spaces, associated ultrafilters, Tukey theory, and Dedekind cuts of nonstandard arithmetic. My master's thesis comes from an earlier period in analysis, studying attractors for nonlinear reaction-diffusion equations in unbounded domains. I keep both here because they show two parts of my mathematical development: one in infinite combinatorics and foundations, and one in analysis and dynamics.

Student Research and Mentoring

Mentoring research is not separate from my research identity. I have worked with students on mathematical logic, set theory, combinatorial set theory, social choice theory, graph theory, modal graph theory, generalized trigonometric systems, numerical integration, mathematical modeling, chaos theory, category theory, hyperobjects, and philosophy of mathematics. The projects vary in topic, but the goal is steady: help students make something mathematical that can be presented, read, revised, and remembered.

Several student projects have moved beyond a course assignment into conference talks or publications. I especially value projects where students learn to ask sharper questions after the first answer appears.
