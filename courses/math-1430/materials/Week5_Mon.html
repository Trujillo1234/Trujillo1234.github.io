1. The Direct Comparison Test

Direct Comparison Test

Assume that there is some $N$ such that for all $n \ge N$: $$ 0 < b_n \le c_n $$
  1. If $\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n$ converges, then $\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} b_n$ also converges.
  2. If $\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} b_n$ diverges, then $\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n$ also diverges.

Proof Sketch:
Consider the partial sums. If $b_n \le c_n$ for all $n$, then: $$ b_0 \le c_0 $$ $$ b_0 + b_1 \le c_0 + c_1 $$ $$ b_0 + b_1 +b_2 \le c_0 + c_1+c_2 $$ $$ b_0 + b_1 +b_2+b_3 \le c_0 + c_1+c_2+c_3 $$ $$ \vdots $$ If the larger series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n$ converges to a sum, the partial sums of the smaller series are bounded above by sum. Since the terms $b_n$ are positive, the sequence of partial sums is increasing. By the Monotonic Sequence Theorem, the series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} b_n$ must also converge.

If the smaller series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} b_n$ diverges, then since the $c_n$ terms are all positive, the sequence of partial sums grows without bound. Thus the series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_{n}$ must also diverge.

2. The Limit Comparison Test

Limit Comparison Test

Suppose that $b_n>0$ and $c_n>0$ for all $n$.

If the limit $\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{b_n}{c_n} $ exists and $$ 0 < \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{b_n}{c_n} < \infty $$ then the two series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} b_n$ and $\displaystyle \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} c_n$ either both converge or both diverge.

Why is this true?
Let $\displaystyle L = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{b_n}{c_n}$. When $n$ is sufficiently large ($n \ge N$), the ratio is approximately $L$: $$ \frac{b_n}{c_n} \approx L \implies b_n \approx L \cdot c_n $$ Since $b_n$ is essentially a constant multiple of $c_n$ in the "long run" (the tail), they behave the same way. If one adds up to a finite number, so does the other.

3. Two Foundational Examples

Example: Determine if the series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2+n}$ converges or diverges.
We use the Direct Comparison Test. We will compare the series to a $p$-series with $p=2$: $$ \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} $$ The $p$-series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2}$ converges because $p = 2 > 1$.

For all $n\ge 1$, $$0< \frac{1}{n^2+n}\le \frac{1}{n^2}$$ since $n^2+n$ is larger than $n^2$.

Thus by the direct comparison test, since the $p$-series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2}$ converges, the series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=1}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2+n}$ must also converges.
Example: Determine if the series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2-n}$ converges or diverges.
We use the Limit Comparison Test. We will compare the series to a $p$-series with $p=2$: $$\displaystyle \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2} $$ The $p$-series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2}$ converges because $p = 2 > 1$. Both series have positive terms for $n \ge 2$.

We define the limit $L$: $$ L = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{\frac{1}{n^2-n}}{\frac{1}{n^2}} = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{1}{n^2-n} \cdot \frac{n^2}{1} $$ $$ L = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{n^2}{n^2-n} $$ Using L'Hôpital's Rule: $$ L = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{2n}{2n-1} = \lim_{n\to\infty} \frac{2}{2} = 1 $$ Thus $0 < L < \infty$.
By the limit comparison test, since the $p$-series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2}$ converges, the series $\displaystyle \sum_{n=2}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n^2-n}$ must also converges.
